% ------- 2.1 Waves -------
\subsection{Waves}

\subsubsection{Properties of Waves}
To describe waves, considering a sinusoidal wave of the form \(A_p\sin(2\pi\nu t)\), we use the following terms:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Peak amplitude} (\(A_p\)): maximum value of the wave from its equilibrium position.
    \item \textbf{Peak-to-peak amplitude}: total height of the wave from its maximum to its minimum value (i.e., \(2A_p\)).
    \item \textbf{Frequency} (\(\nu\)): number of cycles per second (Hz).
    \item \textbf{Time} (\(t\)): time variable.
\end{itemize}
To describe AC signals effectively, we take the root mean square (RMS) amplitude \(\frac{A_p}{\sqrt{2}}\) of values such as current and voltage. This is because the mean value of an AC signal is zero, and the RMS is a more meaningful measure of the signal's amplitude as it gives a positive value that is proportional to the energy in the signal.
\[I_{\text{RMS}} = \frac{I_p}{\sqrt{2}}, \qquad V_{\text{RMS}} = \frac{V_p}{\sqrt{2}}.\]

\subsubsection{Waveforms}
\begin{definition}[Waveform]
    A waveform is a graphical representation of how a signal varies over time, and can take various forms such as a sine wave, square wave, triangle wave, or sawtooth wave.
\end{definition}

\paragraph{1. Direct current (DC).}
A direct current is constant in time, so its graph is a horizontal line.

A pulsating current is an alternating current superimposed on a non-zero DC level. This is just a matter of adding a constant DC level to an AC signal so that the signal is either shifted up or down with respect to the zero current level.

% ------- 2.2 Linear Systems -------
\subsection{Linear Systems}

\begin{definition}[Linear System]
    A system $H$ is linear if it satisfies superposition
    \[H[x_1(t) + x_2(t)] = H[x_1(t)] + H[x_2(t)],\]
    and scaling
    \[H[\alpha x(t)] = \alpha H[x(t)],\]
    for all inputs $x_1(t), x_2(t)$ and constant $\alpha$.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}
    Consider two inputs $x_1(t)$ and $x_2(t)$ to a linear system producing outputs $y_1(t) = H[x_1(t)]$ and $y_2(t) = H[x_2(t)]$, where $H$ is some transformation function.

    A linear transformation must satisfy:
    \[y_{\text{total}} = \alpha y_1(t) + \beta y_2(t) = H[\alpha x_1(t) + \beta x_2(t)],\]
    where \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are constants.
\end{example}

\section{Electromagnetic Theory}

% ------- 3.1 Electric Charge -------
\subsection{Electric Charge}
\begin{definition}
    Charge is a fundamental physical property that comes in two types: positive (+) and negative (--) (which cancel). Opposite charges attract and equal amounts of positive and negative charge cancel, so most matter is electrically neutral. Electric charge is conserved, meaning it cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred. It is also quantized, existing in discrete multiples of the elementary charge
    \[e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C},\]
    where the coulomb (C) is the SI unit of charge.
\end{definition}

% ------- 3.2 Electric Field -------
\subsection{Electric Field}
\begin{definition}[Electric Field]
    The electric field $\vec{E}$ is a vector field produced by electric charges. It describes the force that a charge would experience at any point in space. Quantitatively, the electric field at a point is defined as the force $\vec{F}$ acting on a small positive test charge $q$ placed at that point, divided by the magnitude of the charge:
    \[\vec{E} = \frac{\vec{F}}{q}.\]
\end{definition}
The direction of $\vec{E}$ is the direction of the force on a positive test charge. Its SI units are newtons per coulomb (N/C), which are equivalent to volts per meter (V/m).

% ------- 3.3 Electric Potential -------
\subsection{Electric Potential}
\begin{definition}[Electric Potential]
    The electric potential $V(\vec{r})$ at a point is the electric potential energy per unit charge:
    \[V = \frac{U}{q}.\]
    It is a scalar quantity with units of volts (V) that represents the work done per unit charge to move a positive test charge from a reference point (often taken at infinity) to the point $\vec{r}$, without changing its kinetic energy. It is defined relative to a reference point $\vec{r}_0$ by
    \[V(\vec{r}) - V(\vec{r}_0) = -\int_{\vec{r}_0}^{\vec{r}} \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l},\]
    where $\vec{E}$ is the electric field. The electric field is related to the potential by
    \[\vec{E} = -\nabla V.\]
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[Uniform Electric Field]
    If $\vec{E}$ is uniform, the potential difference between two points separated by $\vec{d}$ is
    \[\Delta V = -\vec{E} \cdot \vec{d}.\]
\end{example}

% ------- 3.4 Electric Potential Energy -------
\subsection{Electric Potential Energy}
\begin{definition}[Electric Potential Energy]
    Electric potential energy $U$ is the energy a charge possesses due to its position in an electric field. For a charge $q$ at a point where the electric potential is $V$, the potential energy is
    \[U = qV,\]
    and is measured in joules (J). Since electric potential is defined relative to a reference point, the potential energy is also defined relative to that same reference.
\end{definition}
An electron placed between two charged plates experiences a potential difference $\Delta V$. The change in its electric potential energy is
\[\Delta U = q\,\Delta V.\]
If the electron is allowed to move freely, this change in electric potential energy is converted into kinetic energy:
\[\Delta K = -\Delta U.\]

% ------- 3.5 Magnetic Field -------
\subsection{Magnetic Field}
\begin{definition}[Magnetic Field]
    A magnetic field $\vec{B}$ is a vector field produced by moving electric charges (currents) and intrinsic magnetic moments of particles. It exerts a force on moving charges given by the Lorentz force law:
    \[\vec{F} = q(\vec{v} \times \vec{B}),\]
    where $q$ is the charge and $\vec{v}$ is its velocity. The force is perpendicular to both the velocity and the magnetic field. Unlike electric fields, which act on charges whether stationary or moving, magnetic fields act only on moving charges. For a long straight current-carrying wire, the magnitude of the magnetic field at a distance $r$ is
    \[B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r},\]
    where $\mu_0$ is the permeability of free space\footnote{The permeability of free space is $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\,\mathrm{T} \cdot \mathrm{m/A}$.} and $I$ is the current. The SI unit of magnetic field is the tesla (T).
\end{definition}

% ------- 3.6 Magnetic Flux -------
\subsection{Magnetic Flux}
\begin{definition}[Magnetic Flux]
    Magnetic flux $\Phi_B$ through a surface is defined as the surface integral of the magnetic field $\vec{B}$ over that surface:
    \[\Phi_B = \int \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{A},\]
    where $d\vec{A}$ is an infinitesimal area element of the surface, and the dot product accounts for the angle between the magnetic field and the normal to the surface. The SI unit of magnetic flux is the weber (Wb).
\end{definition}

% ------- 3.7 Electromagnetic Induction -------
\marginpar{January 22, 2026}
\subsection{Electromagnetic Induction}

\subsubsection{Faraday's Law}
\begin{theorem}[Faraday's Law]
    Faraday's Law states that a changing magnetic flux through a closed loop induces an electromotive force (EMF) in the loop:
    \[\mathcal{E}_{\text{EMF}} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt},\]
    where $\mathcal{E}_{\text{EMF}}$ is the induced EMF and $\Phi_B$ is the magnetic flux through a wire loop. The negative sign indicates that the induced EMF creates a current whose magnetic field opposes the change in flux, as described by Lenz's Law.
\end{theorem}

\subsubsection{Lenz's Law}
\begin{law}[Lenz's Law]
    The induced current in a circuit flows in a direction such that the magnetic field it produces opposes the change in magnetic flux that generated it.
\end{law}
Consider any closed conducting loop. If the magnetic flux through the loop increases, the induced current produces a magnetic field that opposes the increase. If the flux decreases, the induced current produces a field that tends to restore it.

When the magnetic flux is increasing, the induced current is directed opposite to the orientation predicted by the right-hand rule for the increasing field. When the flux is decreasing, the induced current follows the orientation that attempts to reinforce the original field.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[scale=0.5]{../Images/Lenzslaw.png}
    \caption{Direction of the induced current according to Lenz's Law.}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Mutual Inductance}
\begin{definition}[Mutual Inductance]
    Mutual inductance is the property of two nearby circuits whereby a time-varying current in one coil induces an electromotive force (EMF) in another through magnetic flux linkage.
\end{definition}
If a current $I_1(t)$ flows in the primary coil, it produces a magnetic flux $\Phi_{21}$ through the secondary coil. According to Faraday's law,
\[\mathcal{E}_2 = -\frac{d\Phi_{21}}{dt}.\]
The mutual inductance $M$ is defined by
\[\Phi_{21} = M I_1,\]
which gives
\[\mathcal{E}_2 = -M \frac{dI_1}{dt}.\]
The value of $M$ depends on the number of turns in each coil, their geometry, their separation, and the magnetic permeability of the material between them.

\section{Circuit Fundamentals}

% ------- 4.1 Current and Voltage -------
\subsection{Current and Voltage}

\begin{definition}[Electric Current]
    Electric current is the flow of electrons through a wire driven by an electric field potential energy difference. Defined as the rate of charge past a point in a circuit:
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:current}
        I = \frac{dQ}{dt},
    \end{equation}
    and is measured in amperes (A), where $1\,\text{A} = 1\,\text{C/s}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Voltage]
    The voltage (or electric potential difference) is the difference in potential energy between two points in an electric field.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Electron-volt]
    An electron-volt (eV) is a unit of energy equal to the energy gained or lost by an electron when it moves through an electric potential difference of one volt. Since energy is given by $U = qV$, for a charge equal to the elementary charge $e$, we have
    \[1\,\text{eV} = e \cdot 1\,\text{V} = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{J}.\]
\end{definition}

When analyzing circuits, current is defined using the convention of positive charge flow. By convention, current flows from higher potential to lower potential, even though in metallic conductors the actual charge carriers are electrons moving in the opposite direction.

When the circuit is closed, electrons flow externally from the anode to the cathode, while conventional current flows from the cathode to the anode. As the charges move through the circuit, the battery's chemical energy is converted into electrical energy and then into other forms such as heat or light.

% ------- 4.2 Electromotive Force -------
\marginpar{January 15, 2026}
\subsection{Electromotive Force}

\begin{definition}[Electromotive Force (EMF)]
    Electromotive Force (EMF)\footnote{EMF is the total energy supplied per unit charge by a source. The terminal voltage across a real source is typically less than the EMF due to internal resistance.} is the energy supplied per unit charge by an energy source such as a battery or generator. It represents the work done by the source to move a charge around a complete circuit. EMF is measured in volts (V).
\end{definition}

To better understand the concept of EMF, consider the following analogy: EMF is like a voltage credit that is then all spent by dropping it over the elements of the circuit. Think of your checking account with two columns: credit (money in) and debit (money spent). EMF is the ``credit'' that is then all ``spent'' by dropping it over the elements of the circuit.

% ------- 4.3 Ohm's Law -------
\subsection{Ohm's Law}

\begin{definition}[Ohm's Law]
    Ohm's Law states that the current \(I\) flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to the voltage \(V\) across it and inversely proportional to its resistance \(R\):
    \[I = \frac{V}{R}.\]
\end{definition}

Microscopically, Ohm's Law can be understood in terms of the motion of electrons in a conductor. When a voltage is applied across a conductor, it creates an electric field that exerts a force on the free electrons, causing them to move and create an electric current. The resistance of the conductor arises from collisions between the electrons and the atoms in the material, which impede their motion.

The current density $J$ is related to the electric field strength $E$ by
\[J = \sigma E, \qquad E = J\rho_r,\]
where $\sigma$ is the conductivity and $\rho_r$ is the resistivity. The current is
\[I = AJ = A\sigma E.\]
Using $E = \Delta V/l$,
\[I = \frac{A\sigma\,\Delta V}{l} = \frac{A\,\Delta V}{\rho_r l}.\]
From Ohm's Law, $\Delta V = IR$, which gives the resistance in terms of material properties:
\[R = \frac{\rho_r l}{A}.\]

Ohm's Law can be represented graphically using the Ohm's Law Wheel, which illustrates the relationships between current, voltage, resistance, and conductance.

\end{figure}

% ------- 4.4 Kirchhoff's Laws -------
\subsection{Kirchhoff's Laws}

\begin{definition}[Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL)]
    The algebraic sum of all potential differences around any closed loop in a circuit is equal to zero.
\end{definition}

KVL allows us to determine the potential difference between two points in a circuit by accounting for the voltage changes across each component along the path.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{../Images/kirchhoffrule.jpg}
    \caption{Each resistor and voltage source is traversed from $a$ to $b$. The corresponding potential changes across each element are indicated. (The script $\mathcal{E}$ denotes the emf.)}
    \label{klawformula}
\end{figure}

Another common circuit element is the inductor. In the context of KVL, the voltage across an inductor is included in the loop equation in the same way as the voltage across resistors or capacitors.

\begin{definition}[Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL)]
    The sum of the currents entering a node is equal to the sum of the currents leaving the node.
\end{definition}

% ------- 4.5 Direct and Alternating Current -------
\subsection{Direct and Alternating Current}

\begin{definition}[Direct Current (DC)]
    Direct Current (DC) is a form of current where voltage and current are constant over time; commonly found in batteries.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[DC Offset]
    A DC offset is the addition of a constant DC value to an AC signal. This shifts the entire signal up or down relative to the \(0\,\text{V}\) level, without changing the shape of the AC signal.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Alternating Current (AC)]
    Alternating Current (AC) is a form of current that changes over time, often in a sinusoidal manner. AC currents are commonly used in power distribution systems, such as household electrical outlets.
\end{definition}

In general, an alternating current source is represented in circuit diagrams by a circular source symbol containing a sinusoidal waveform.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{../Images/AC_symbol.png}
    \caption{Alternating Current symbol.}
\end{figure}

If the current is sinusoidal, it is commonly written as
\[I(t) = I_0\sin(\omega t),\]
where $I_0$ is the amplitude and $\omega$ is the angular frequency.

If the wire has a small but non-negligible resistance that is uniformly distributed, the electric potential decreases approximately linearly with distance, since the voltage drop across a uniform conductor is proportional to its length.

% ------- 4.7 Circuit Classification -------
\subsection{Circuit Classification}

Circuits may be classified according to whether they are linear or non-linear, and whether they contain active or passive elements. A linear circuit element has a linear (straight-line) relationship between voltage and current, meaning $V \propto I$; a non-linear element does not.

\begin{definition}[Passive Linear Circuit]
    Contains only passive linear elements, such as resistors, capacitors, inductors, and transformers.
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Active Linear Circuit]
    Contains at least one active element (e.g.\ transistor, op-amp, or independent source) while maintaining an overall linear $V$--$I$ relationship.
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Passive Non-linear Circuit]
    Contains only passive components but includes at least one non-linear element, such as a diode.
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Active Non-linear Circuit]
    Contains at least one active element and at least one non-linear element.
\end{definition}

\section{Circuit Components}

% ------- 5.1 Resistors -------
\subsection{Resistors}

\begin{definition}
    A resistor is a device that opposes the flow of current through it, resulting in a voltage drop across it. Resistors are characterized by their resistance value \(R\), measured in ohms (\(\Omega\)).
\end{definition}

When resistors are connected in series, their resistances add: $R_{\text{tot}} = R_1 + R_2 + \cdots + R_n$. In parallel, they add inversely: $\frac{1}{R_{\text{tot}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \cdots + \frac{1}{R_n}$.

\begin{example}
    \begin{figure}[H]
        \begin{center}
            \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{../Images/Parallel_resistors.png}
            \caption{Examples of components wired in parallel.}
        \end{center}
    \end{figure}
\end{example}

\subsubsection{Temperature Dependence}
The resistance of many metals varies with temperature as
\[R_T = R_0\left[1 + \alpha(T - T_0)\right],\]
where \(R_0\) is the resistance at reference temperature \(T_0\) and \(\alpha\) is the linear temperature coefficient.

\subsubsection{Power Dissipation}
When current flows through a resistor, electrical energy is converted into heat. The power \(P\) dissipated is
\[P = \Delta V \cdot I = I^2 R = \frac{(\Delta V)^2}{R}.\]
The total energy dissipated over time is
\[U = \int R\,I^2(t)\,dt.\]

% ------- 5.2 Batteries -------
\subsection{Batteries}

\begin{definition}[Battery]
    A battery converts stored chemical energy into electrical energy by maintaining a potential difference between its terminals. Inside the battery, chemical reactions separate charge, creating an excess of negative charge at the anode and positive charge at the cathode.
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Ideal Battery]
    An ideal battery is a voltage source with zero internal resistance that maintains a constant voltage regardless of the current drawn.
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Real Battery]
    A real battery has internal resistance $r$ that causes the terminal voltage to decrease as the current increases, leading to energy loss as heat.
\end{definition}

A real battery is modelled as an ideal voltage source \(V_0\) in series with an internal resistance \(r\). The terminal voltage when current \(I\) is drawn is
\[V_{\text{terminal}} = V_0 - Ir.\]
If an external resistor $R$ is connected, Ohm's Law gives
\[V_0 = Ir + IR.\]

% ------- 5.3 Ground and Reference -------
\subsection{Ground and Reference}

\begin{definition}[Circuit Ground]
    The reference point in a circuit at which the electric potential is defined to be zero. All voltages in the circuit are measured relative to this point. It does not have to be physically connected to the Earth. Symbol: \begin{circuitikz}\draw (0,0) node[ground]{};\end{circuitikz}.
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Chassis Ground]
    A conductive frame or enclosure of a device used as a common return path for current; often connected to circuit ground. It provides shielding and safety by preventing exposed metal parts from reaching dangerous voltages. Symbol: \begin{circuitikz}\draw (0,0) node[sground]{};\end{circuitikz}.
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Earth Ground]
    A physical connection to the Earth, typically made using a metal rod or pipe driven into the soil, used primarily for safety to allow fault currents to safely dissipate.
\end{definition}

% ------- 5.4 Capacitors -------
\subsection{Capacitors}

\begin{definition}[Capacitor]
    A capacitor is a passive electrical component that stores energy in an electric field between two conductive plates separated by an insulating material (dielectric) or space. The capacitance \(C\) is defined as the ratio of the charge \(Q\) stored on one plate to the voltage \(V\) across the plates:
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:capacitance}
        C = \frac{Q}{V}.
    \end{equation}
    The SI unit of capacitance is the farad (F).
\end{definition}

For a parallel plate capacitor,
\[C = \frac{\varepsilon A}{d},\]
where \(\varepsilon\) is the dielectric constant, \(A\) is the area of the plates, and \(d\) is the distance between them. The permittivity of free space is
\[\varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12}\,\text{F/m}.\]

Capacitors combine oppositely to resistors:
\[C_{\text{tot}} = C_1 + C_2 + \cdots + C_n \quad \text{(parallel)}, \qquad \frac{1}{C_{\text{tot}}} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} + \cdots + \frac{1}{C_n} \quad \text{(series)}.\]

\subsubsection{Dielectric Insulator}
\begin{definition}[Dielectric Insulator]
    A dielectric is an insulating material that permits an electric field but does not conduct charge. When placed between capacitor plates, it increases capacitance by reducing the effective electric field, allowing more charge to be stored for a given voltage. Its permittivity is
    \[\varepsilon = \varepsilon_r\,\varepsilon_0,\]
    where $\varepsilon_r$ is the relative permittivity.
\end{definition}

\subsubsection{Energy Stored in a Capacitor}
The energy stored equals the work required to move charge from one plate to the other. Since $V = Q/C$,
\[W = \int_0^Q V\,dQ = \int_0^Q \frac{Q}{C}\,dQ = \frac{1}{2}CV^2.\]

\subsubsection{Charging a Capacitor}
From \eqref{eq:capacitance} and \eqref{eq:current}, the current-voltage relationship for a capacitor is
\[I = C\frac{dV}{dt}, \qquad V(t) = \frac{1}{C}\int I\,dt + V_0.\]
For constant current,
\[V(t) = V_0 + \frac{I}{C}\,t,\]
so the voltage increases linearly with slope $I/C$.

    \begin{tikzpicture}
        \draw[->] (0,0) -- (5,0) node[right] {$t$};
        \draw[->] (0,0) -- (0,4) node[above] {$V$};
        \draw (0,1) node[left] {$V_0$} -- (0.1,1);
        \draw (0,1) -- (4,3);
        \node at (4,2.6) {$\frac{I}{C}$};
    \end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

% ------- 5.5 Inductors -------
\subsection{Inductors}

\begin{definition}[Inductor]
    An inductor is a passive electrical component that stores energy in a magnetic field created by the flow of current through a coil of wire. The inductance \(L\) of an inductor is defined as the ratio of the magnetic flux \(\Phi\) linking the coil to the current \(I\) flowing through it:
    \[L = \frac{\Phi}{I}.\]
    The SI unit of inductance is the henry (H).
\end{definition}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.3\textwidth]{../Images/Inductor_symbols.png}

\subsubsection{Energy Storage}
Energy is stored in the magnetic field within the inductor coil. For a constant applied voltage, the current in the coil rises linearly with slope $V/L$:
\begin{center}
    \begin{tikzpicture}
        \draw[->] (0,0) -- (5,0) node[right] {$t$};

The energy stored is
\[W = \int_0^t V\cdot i\,dt = \int L\frac{di}{dt}\cdot i\,dt = \frac{1}{2}LI^2.\]

\subsubsection{Networks of Inductors}

\paragraph{Inductors in Series.}
The equivalent inductance is
\[L_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} L_i.\]

\paragraph{Inductors in Parallel.}
The equivalent inductance satisfies
\[\frac{1}{L_{\text{total}}} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{1}{L_i}.\]

These relations are formally identical to those of resistors, but arise from the inductor voltage-current relation.

\subsubsection{Solenoid}
\begin{definition}[Solenoid]
    A solenoid is a long cylindrical coil of wire designed to produce a nearly uniform magnetic field inside it when current flows.
\end{definition}
For a solenoid of length $l$, cross-sectional area $A = \pi r^2$, number of turns $N$, and core permeability $\mu$,
\[L = \frac{\mu N^2 A}{l}.\]

\subsection{Transformers \& Mutual Inductance (Not Tested)}

\begin{definition}[Transformer]
    A transformer is a device consisting of two coils of wire (primary and secondary) magnetically coupled through a shared core. Its main purpose is to transfer energy between circuits to change voltage levels. Symbol:
    \begin{center}
        \begin{circuitikz}
            \draw (0,0) node[transformer] (T) {}
            (T.A1) -- ++(-1,0)
            (T.A2) -- ++(-1,0)
            (T.B1) -- ++(1,0)
            (T.B2) -- ++(1,0);
        \end{circuitikz}
    \end{center}
\end{definition}

A transformer has $N_1$ and $N_2$ turns wound around a common core. A time-varying current $I_1$ in the primary produces flux $\Phi_B$ through the secondary. By Faraday's law,
\[\mathcal{E}_2 = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = -M\frac{dI_1}{dt}.\]

\begin{center}
    \begin{figure}[H]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{../Images/Transformer.png}
        \caption{Visual representation of a transformer.}
    \end{figure}
\end{center}

The SI unit of mutual inductance is the henry (H). If $I_1(t) = I_0\sin(\omega t)$, then
\[V_2(t) = M\omega I_0\cos(\omega t),\]
so the secondary voltage is sinusoidal but shifted in phase by $\pi/2$ relative to the current.

For an ideal transformer, power is conserved ($V_1 I_1 = V_2 I_2$), giving the transformer relations:
\[\frac{V_1}{V_2} = \frac{N_1}{N_2}, \qquad \frac{I_1}{I_2} = \frac{N_2}{N_1}.\]

\begin{remark}
    Long-distance power lines use high-voltage AC because it significantly reduces energy loss during transmission: higher voltage means lower current, which minimises resistive heat loss. Transformers efficiently step AC voltage up and down as needed.
\end{remark}

% ------- 6.2 RC Circuit with AC Driving -------
\marginpar{January 29, 2026}
\subsection{RC Circuit with AC Driving}

\subsubsection{Driving Frequency and Time Constant}
For a periodic input with period $T$ and angular frequency $\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T} = 2\pi\nu$, the time constant for an RC circuit is
\[\tau_{RC} = RC.\]
The circuit behaviour depends on the ratio of $T$ to $\tau$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Low frequency: $T \gg \tau$
    \item High frequency: $T \ll \tau$
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{RC Circuit with Square-Wave Input}

\subsubsection*{Low frequency ($T \gg \tau$)}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Capacitor fully charges and discharges each half-cycle.
    \item Voltage drops almost entirely across the capacitor.
    \item Current is nearly zero (except for spikes at each voltage jump).
    \item Capacitor behaves approximately like an open circuit (break).
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{High frequency ($T \ll \tau$)}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Capacitor does not have time to charge.
    \item Very little voltage is dropped across the capacitor.
    \item Current values are close to what we expect for a pure resistor circuit; voltage profile is triangle-wave-like.
    \item Capacitor behaves approximately like a short circuit.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Integrator and Differentiator Behaviour}

\subsubsection*{Low frequency}
At low frequency, the capacitor has time to fully charge, so its voltage tracks the input: $\Delta V_C(t) \approx V(t)$. The current is therefore $I \approx C\frac{dV}{dt}$, and the voltage across the resistor is
\[\Delta V_R(t) = R\frac{dQ}{dt} \approx \tau\frac{dV_{\text{in}}(t)}{dt}, \qquad \tau = RC.\]
The resistor acts as a differentiator. Sharp transitions in the square-wave input produce current spikes.

\subsubsection*{High frequency}
At high frequency, the capacitor has no time to charge, so the resistor voltage tracks the input: $\Delta V_R(t) \approx V(t)$, giving $I \approx V(t)/R$. The capacitor voltage is then
\[\Delta V_C(t) = \frac{Q}{C} \approx \frac{1}{\tau}\int_0^t V_{\text{in}}(t')\,dt', \qquad \tau = RC.\]
The capacitor acts as an integrator. A square wave input produces a triangle-wave-like voltage across the capacitor.

% ------- 6.3 LR Circuit -------
\subsection{LR Circuit}

\begin{definition}[LR Circuit]
    An LR circuit is a circuit in which an inductor is connected in series with a resistor.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}
    Consider this circuit at the moment it is turned on (switch connects to `A'). If the inductor were replaced with a short, a current $V/R$ would flow immediately, with all of the voltage drop across $R$.
    \begin{figure}[H]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{../Images/LR_circuit.png}
        \caption{Visual representation of an LR circuit.}
        \label{LR-circuit}
    \end{figure}
    The current flow produces a magnetic flux through the coil. By Lenz's law, the coil induces a voltage equal to the battery's voltage, opposing the change in flux. Therefore, just at the instant the switch is closed, the current flow is zero.
\end{example}

For a single coil, the voltage-current relationship is
\[V = L\frac{di}{dt},\]
where $L$ is the self-inductance. The characteristic time constant is $\tau = L/R$.

\subsubsection{DC Analysis}

\subsubsection*{Charging}
When the switch is connected to terminal A,
\[I(t) = \frac{V}{R}\!\left(1 - e^{-t/\tau}\right), \qquad V_L(t) = V\,e^{-t/\tau}.\]
At $t = 0$, an inductor behaves like an open circuit. As $t \to \infty$, it behaves like a short circuit and carries the full steady-state current $V/R$.

\subsubsection*{Discharging}
\[V_L(t) = V_0\,e^{-t/\tau}, \qquad I(t) = I_0\,e^{-t/\tau}.\]
Both current and voltage decay exponentially to zero.

% ------- 6.4 LR Circuit with AC Driving -------
\subsection{LR Circuit with AC Driving}

\subsubsection{Driving Frequency and Time Constant}
For a periodic input with period $T$ and angular frequency $\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T} = 2\pi\nu$, the time constant for an LR circuit is
\[\tau_{LR} = \frac{L}{R}.\]
The circuit behaviour depends on the ratio of $T$ to $\tau$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Low frequency: $T \gg \tau$
    \item High frequency: $T \ll \tau$
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{LR Circuit with Square-Wave Input}

\subsubsection*{Low frequency}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Inductor fully responds each half-cycle.
    \item Voltage drops almost entirely across the resistor.
    \item Current closely follows the input and is approximately square-wave-like.
    \item Inductor behaves approximately like a short circuit.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{High frequency}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Inductor does not have time to respond.
    \item Voltage drops almost entirely across the inductor.
    \item Current changes very little; the resistor voltage profile consists of sharp spikes at each transition.
    \item Inductor behaves approximately like an open circuit.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Integrator and Differentiator Behaviour}

\subsubsection*{Low frequency}
At low frequency, the inductor has time to fully respond, so the resistor voltage tracks the input: $\Delta V_R(t) \approx V(t)$, giving $I \approx V_{\text{in}}(t)/R$. The voltage across the inductor is then
\[\Delta V_L(t) = L\frac{dI}{dt} \approx \tau\frac{dV_{\text{in}}(t)}{dt}, \qquad \tau = \frac{L}{R}.\]
The inductor acts as a differentiator. Sharp transitions in the square-wave input produce voltage spikes across the inductor.

\subsubsection*{High frequency}
At high frequency, the inductor has no time to respond, so its voltage tracks the input: $\Delta V_L(t) \approx V(t)$, giving $L\frac{dI}{dt} \approx V_{\text{in}}(t)$. The voltage across the resistor is then
\[\Delta V_R(t) = IR \approx \frac{1}{\tau}\int_0^t V_{\text{in}}(t')\,dt', \qquad \tau = \frac{L}{R}.\]
The resistor acts as an integrator. A square-wave input produces a triangle-wave-like voltage across the resistor.

\subsection{Frequency Behaviour Summary}

\begin{itemize}

    \item An inductor passes DC (after long time) and resists changes in current.

    \hline
    \end{tabular}
\end{center}

\section{Phasors and Complex Impedance}
\marginpar{February 5, 2026}

% ------- 7.1 Complex Numbers and AC Signals -------
\subsection{Complex Representation of AC Signals}

A complex signal is defined as
\[\tilde{V}(t) = V_0 e^{j\phi(t)} = V_0 e^{j(\omega t + \phi_0)}.\]
This complex signal is not physical, but it embeds the real-valued physical signal $V(t)$ as its real part:
\[V(t) = V_0\cos(\phi(t)) = \operatorname{Re}\!\left[V_0 e^{j\phi(t)}\right] = \operatorname{Re}\!\left[\tilde{V}(t)\right].\]

% ------- 7.2 Working with Complex Signals -------
\subsection{Working with Complex Signals}

To analyze an AC circuit using complex signals, perform circuit analysis\footnote{Circuit analysis is the process of determining the voltages and currents throughout a circuit by applying circuit laws (KVL, KCL) and component relationships. For AC circuits, this is done using the complex signal $\tilde{V}(t)$ in place of the real signal, which simplifies differentiation and integration into algebraic operations.} with $\tilde{V}(t)$ to obtain a complex output signal $\tilde{V}_{\text{out}}(t)$. The real-valued physical output is then recovered by taking the real part:
\[V_{\text{out}}(t) = \operatorname{Re}\!\left[\tilde{V}_{\text{out}}(t)\right].\]
Alternatively, one can extract directly the change in signal amplitude and phase shift between input and output.

% ------- 7.3 Capacitor Current -------
\subsection{Capacitor Current}

Consider a voltage signal $V(t) = \operatorname{Re}[\tilde{V}(t)]$ dropped across a capacitor. To find the current $I(t)$, we first compute the complex current $\tilde{I}(t)$ arising from $\tilde{V}(t)$, then take the real part:
\[\tilde{I}(t) = \frac{d\tilde{Q}}{dt} = C\frac{d\tilde{V}(t)}{dt} = j C\omega V_0 e^{j(\omega t + \phi_0)} = j C\omega\tilde{V}.\]
Extracting $I(t)$ using the fact that $j = e^{j\pi/2}$:
\[I(t) = \operatorname{Re}\!\left[\tilde{I}(t)\right] = C\omega V_0\operatorname{Re}\!\left[e^{j(\omega t + \phi_0 + \pi/2)}\right] = C\omega V_0\cos\!\left(\omega t + \phi_0 + \tfrac{\pi}{2}\right).\]
Note that $I$ vanishes in the limit $\omega \to 0$ (DC limit), since in steady state the capacitor fully charges and fully opposes the voltage source. From the expression above, the current leads the input voltage by $\pi/2$; equivalently, the input voltage lags the current by $\pi/2$.

% ------- 7.4 Impedance of a Capacitor -------
\subsection{Impedance of a Capacitor}

Define the capacitive impedance
\[Z_C = \frac{1}{j\omega C} \implies \tilde{I} = \frac{\tilde{V}}{Z_C},\]
which is the complex generalization of Ohm's Law. The quantity $Z$ is a complex resistance called impedance. The capacitive impedance is very large at low $\omega$ and decreases as $\omega$ increases. In the DC limit $\omega \to 0$, $Z_C \to \infty$, which is equivalent to saying the capacitor blocks DC signals. The complex character of the impedance encodes how the capacitor affects the phase offset between input and output signals.

% ------- 7.5 Impedance Table -------
\subsection{Impedance}

An analogous calculation for an inductor and resistor yields the following impedance table:
\begin{center}
    \begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|}
    \hline
    Component & Impedance ($Z$) & Phase offset of current relative to voltage \\
    \hline
    Resistor   & $R$                    & $0$ \\
    \hline
    Capacitor  & $\dfrac{1}{j\omega C}$ & $+\pi/2$ \quad (current leads voltage) \\
    \hline
    Inductor   & $j\omega L$            & $-\pi/2$ \quad (current lags voltage) \\
    \hline
    \end{tabular}
\end{center}

% ------- 7.6 Phase Intuition -------
\subsection{Phase Intuition}
\marginpar{February 10, 2026}

A capacitor causes current to lead voltage by $\pi/2$ because the current flowing into the capacitor must first accumulate charge on its plates before a voltage difference can be established. Since $I = C\frac{dV}{dt}$, maximum current flows when the voltage is changing fastest (crossing zero) and zero current flows when the voltage is stable at its peak.

An inductor causes current to lag voltage because its magnetic properties, governed by Lenz's law, oppose changes in current flow ($V = L\frac{dI}{dt}$), causing the current waveform to reach its maximum $90°$ after the voltage waveform. Voltage is proportional to the rate of change of current, meaning maximum voltage occurs when current changes fastest (at zero crossing) and zero voltage occurs when the current is constant (at its peak), forcing a $90°$ phase shift.

% ------- 7.7 Power Dissipation of AC Signals -------
\subsection{Power Dissipation of AC Signals}

The power dissipated by a circuit element driven by an AC signal is found by averaging over one cycle:
\[P = \frac{1}{T}\int_{0}^{T} I(t)\,\Delta V(t)\,dt.\]
Taking $V(t) = V_0\cos(\omega t)$ and $I(t) = I_0\cos(\omega t + \phi_0)$, this gives
\[P = \frac{V_0 I_0}{T}\int_{0}^{T}\cos(\omega t)\cos(\omega t + \phi_0)\,dt = \frac{V_0 I_0}{2}\cos(\phi_0) = V_{\text{rms}}\,I_{\text{rms}}\cos(\phi_0).\]
For a capacitor and an inductor, $\phi_0 = \pm\pi/2$, so $P = 0$; they do not dissipate energy but instead store and release it. For a resistor, $\phi_0 = 0$, so $P = V_{\text{rms}}\,I_{\text{rms}}$; all the energy is dissipated as heat.

% ------- 7.8 Adding Impedances -------
\subsection{Adding Impedances}

Impedances combine exactly as resistances do:
\[Z_{\text{tot}} = Z_1 + Z_2 \quad \text{(series)}, \qquad Z_{\text{tot}} = \left(\frac{1}{Z_1} + \frac{1}{Z_2}\right)^{-1} \quad \text{(parallel)}.\]

% ------- 7.9 Worked Example: Current in an RC Circuit -------
\subsection{Sinusoidally Driven RC Circuit}

\begin{example}[Current in Sinusoidally Driven RC Circuit]
    Find the current in the following RC circuit:
    \begin{center}
        \begin{tikzpicture}[transform shape]
            \draw (4, 3) to[sinusoidal voltage source] (4, 2.25);
            \draw (4.75, 4.25) to[american resistor] (7, 4.25);
            \draw (8, 4.25) to[capacitor] (8, 1.25);
            \node[sground] at (6, 1.25){};
            \draw (8, 1.25) |- (4, 1.25) -| (4, 2.25);
            \draw (7, 4.25) -- (8, 4.25);
            \draw (4.75, 4.25) |- (4, 4.25) -| (4, 3);
        \end{tikzpicture}
    \end{center}

    We have $\tilde{V}(t) = V_0 e^{j\omega t}$, $Z_R = R$, and $Z_C = \frac{1}{j\omega C}$. The total impedance is
    \[Z_{\text{tot}} = Z_R + Z_C = R + \frac{1}{j\omega C} = \frac{1 + j\omega RC}{j\omega C} = \frac{1+j\omega\tau}{j\omega C},\]
    where $\tau = RC$. The complex current is
    \[\tilde{I}(t) = \frac{\tilde{V}(t)}{Z_{\text{tot}}} = \frac{j\omega C\, V_0\, e^{j\omega t}}{1 + j\omega\tau}.\]

    \paragraph{Amplitude.}
    The magnitude of the total impedance is
    \[|Z_{\text{tot}}|^2 = R^2 + \frac{1}{\omega^2 C^2} = R^2\,\frac{1 + (\omega\tau)^2}{(\omega\tau)^2},\]
    so the current amplitude is
    \[|\tilde{I}| = \frac{V_0}{|Z_{\text{tot}}|} = \frac{V_0}{R}\cdot\frac{\omega\tau}{\sqrt{1+(\omega\tau)^2}}.\]
    At $\omega = 0$ (DC limit) the current vanishes; as $\omega\to\infty$ it rises to $I_{\max} = V_0/R$, since the capacitive impedance vanishes.

    \paragraph{Phase offset.}
    The phase of the current leads the voltage by
    \[\phi = \arctan\!\left(\frac{1}{\omega\tau}\right).\]
    The full complex current is therefore
    \[\tilde{I}(t) = \frac{V_0}{R}\cdot\frac{\omega\tau}{\sqrt{1+(\omega\tau)^2}}\,e^{j(\omega t + \phi)}.\]

    \paragraph{Limiting cases.}
    In the DC limit ($\omega\to 0$), the voltage drops almost entirely across the capacitor and the current leads the voltage by $\phi = \arctan(\infty) = \pi/2$. In the high-frequency limit ($\omega\to\infty$), the voltage drops almost entirely across the resistor and the current is in phase with the voltage: $\phi = \arctan(0) = 0$.
\end{example}
% ------- 7.10 Impedance Divider -------
\subsection{Impedance Divider}
\marginpar{February 12, 2026}

Consider a general voltage divider circuit built from two impedances $Z_1$ and $Z_2$ in series, driven by a sinusoidal source. The output voltage $\tilde{V}'$ is taken across $Z_2$:
\begin{center}
    \begin{tikzpicture}[transform shape]
        \draw (3.5, 0) to[sinusoidal current source] (3.5, 1.75);
        \draw (3.5, 1.75) -| (3.5, 2) -- (6, 2);
        \node[shape=rectangle, draw, line width=1pt, minimum width=0.965cm, minimum height=0.465cm] at (6.5, 2){};
        \draw (8, 2) -- (7, 2);
        \node[shape=rectangle, draw, line width=1pt, minimum width=0.465cm, minimum height=0.965cm] at (8, 1){};
        \draw (3.5, 0) -- (8, 0) -| (8, 0.5);
        \draw (8, 1.5) -| (8, 2);
        \node[sground] at (6, 0){};
        \node[anchor=center, align=center] at (6.5, 2){$Z_1$};
        \node[anchor=center, align=center] at (8, 1){$Z_2$};
    \end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}
The transfer function is
\[\frac{\tilde{V}'}{\tilde{V}} = \frac{Z_2}{Z_1 + Z_2} = \left|\frac{\tilde{V}'}{\tilde{V}}\right|e^{j\phi},\]
where $\left|\frac{\tilde{V}'}{\tilde{V}}\right|$ describes how the output amplitude is affected relative to the input amplitude, and $\phi$ is the phase shift of the output relative to the input.

% ------- 7.11 Filter Examples -------
\subsection{Filter Examples}

\begin{example}[RC Low-Pass Filter]
Consider the following circuit, with output taken across the capacitor:
\begin{center}
    \begin{tikzpicture}[transform shape]
        \draw (5, 0) to[sinusoidal current source] (5, 2);
        \draw (5, 0) -- (9, 0);
        \node[sground] at (7, 0){};
        \draw (9, 0) to[capacitor] (9, 2);
        \draw (9, 2) to[american resistor] (5, 2);
        \node[anchor=south] at (7, 2.1){$Z_1 = R$};
        \node[anchor=center] at (9.8, 1){$Z_2 = \dfrac{1}{j\omega C}$};
        \node[shape=circle, draw, line width=1pt, minimum width=0.1cm] at (9, 2){};
        \node[anchor=west] at (9.15, 2.25){$\tilde{V}'$};
    \end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}
The transfer function is
\[\frac{\tilde{V}'}{\tilde{V}} = \frac{1/j\omega C}{R + 1/j\omega C} = \frac{1}{1 + j\omega\tau},\]
where $\tau = RC$. Expanding,
\[\frac{1}{1+j\omega\tau} = \frac{1 - j\omega\tau}{1+(\omega\tau)^2} = \frac{1}{1+(\omega\tau)^2} - j\frac{\omega\tau}{1+(\omega\tau)^2}.\]
The amplitude ratio and phase shift are therefore

\subsection{Charging with a Step Voltage}

Consider the following series RLC circuit, with a switch closed at $t = 0$ to create a voltage step:
\begin{center}

        \node[anchor=center] at (-2.85, -0.25){\scriptsize $V_0$};
    \end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

Summing voltage drops around the loop (KVL) gives
\[V_0 = RI + L\frac{dI}{dt} + \frac{Q}{C}.\]
Differentiating with respect to time and dividing through by $L$,
\[\frac{d^2I}{dt^2} + \frac{R}{L}\frac{dI}{dt} + \frac{1}{LC}I = 0.\]
We define the natural resonant frequency
\[\omega_0 \equiv \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}},\]
at which the inductive and capacitive reactances are equal in magnitude. At this frequency, energy oscillates efficiently between the inductor and capacitor, the circuit is purely resistive, and the impedance is minimised. We also define the damping factor
\[\Gamma \equiv \frac{R}{L},\]
which represents the rate at which energy is dissipated by the resistor. The equation of motion becomes
\[\frac{d^2I}{dt^2} + \Gamma\frac{dI}{dt} + \omega_0^2\,I = 0.\]

\subsection{Solution by Characteristic Equation}

Substituting the trial solution $I(t) = ce^{pt}$ gives the characteristic equation
\[p^2 + \Gamma p + \omega_0^2 = 0 \implies p = -\frac{\Gamma}{2} \pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{\Gamma}{2}\right)^2 - \omega_0^2}.\]
The nature of the solution depends on the sign of the discriminant, yielding three distinct cases.

\subsection{Damping Cases}

\paragraph{Case 1: Underdamped ($\Gamma < 2\omega_0$).}
The discriminant is negative, giving complex roots. The solution is a sinusoid with exponentially decaying envelope:
\[I(t) = \frac{V_0}{L\omega_f}\sin(\omega_f t)\,e^{-\Gamma t/2},\]
where the damped frequency is
\[\omega_f = \sqrt{\omega_0^2 - \left(\frac{\Gamma}{2}\right)^2}.\]
This is the most physically interesting case, exhibiting oscillatory behaviour before settling. As $R \to 0$, damping vanishes, $\omega_f \to \omega_0$, and in the unphysical limit of a pure LC circuit the oscillation persists indefinitely at the natural frequency $\omega_0$.

\paragraph{Case 2: Overdamped ($\Gamma > 2\omega_0$).}
The discriminant is positive, giving two distinct real roots. The solution decays without oscillation:
\[I(t) = \frac{V_0}{L\omega_f'}\sinh(\omega_f' t)\,e^{-\Gamma t/2},\]
where
\[\omega_f' = \sqrt{\left(\frac{\Gamma}{2}\right)^2 - \omega_0^2}.\]

\paragraph{Case 3: Critically damped ($\Gamma = 2\omega_0$).}
The discriminant is zero, giving a repeated real root. The solution is
\[I(t) = \frac{V_0}{L}\,t\,e^{-\Gamma t/2}.\]
This case represents the fastest return to equilibrium without oscillation.

\begin{center}
    \begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|}
    \hline
    Case & Condition & Behaviour \\
    \hline
    Underdamped   & $\Gamma < 2\omega_0$ & Oscillatory, decaying envelope \\
    \hline
    Critically damped & $\Gamma = 2\omega_0$ & Fastest non-oscillatory return \\
    \hline
    Overdamped    & $\Gamma > 2\omega_0$ & Slow, non-oscillatory decay \\
    \hline
    \end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsection{RLC Circuit with AC Driving}
\marginpar{February 19, 2026}

Consider the following series RLC circuit driven by a sinusoidal voltage source $V(t) = V_0\cos(\omega t)$:
\begin{center}

        \draw (-2.25, -1.5) -- (2.75, -1.5);
        \draw (-2.25, -1.5) to[sinusoidal current source] (-2.25, 2);
        \node[anchor=east] at (-2.4, 0.25){$V(t) = V_0\cos(\omega t)$};
        \node[currarrow, rotate=-90] at (2.75, 0.75){};
        \node[anchor=west] at (2.9, 0.75){$I(t)$};
    \end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

Using the complex representation $\tilde{V} = V_0 e^{j\omega t}$, the total impedance of the series circuit is
\[Z_{\text{tot}} = R + j\omega L + \frac{1}{j\omega C} = R\left(1 - \frac{\omega^2}{\omega_0^2} + j\omega\tau\right)\frac{1}{j\omega\tau}\cdot j\omega\tau,\]
so that the complex current is
\[\tilde{I} = \frac{\tilde{V}}{Z_{\text{tot}}} = \frac{\tilde{V}}{R}\cdot\frac{j\omega\tau}{1 - \dfrac{\omega^2}{\omega_0^2} + j\omega\tau},\]
where $\tau = L/R$ and $\omega_0 = 1/\sqrt{LC}$.

\subsubsection{Amplitude}
\[|\tilde{I}|^2 = \left(\frac{V_0}{R}\right)^2 \frac{(\omega\tau)^2}{\left(1 - \dfrac{\omega^2}{\omega_0^2}\right)^2 + (\omega\tau)^2}.\]
At $\omega = \omega_0$ the denominator reduces to $(\omega_0\tau)^2$, so $|\tilde{I}|$ reaches its maximum
\[I_{\max} = \frac{V_0}{R},\]
the frequency at which the circuit is maximally transmittant. At resonance, the capacitive and inductive impedances cancel exactly, leaving only $R$.

\subsubsection{Phase}
Multiplying numerator and denominator of $\tilde{I}/(\tilde{V}/R)$ by the complex conjugate of the denominator gives
\[\frac{\tilde{I}}{\tilde{V}/R} = \frac{(\omega\tau)^2 + j\omega\tau\!\left(1 - \dfrac{\omega^2}{\omega_0^2}\right)}{\left(1 - \dfrac{\omega^2}{\omega_0^2}\right)^2 + (\omega\tau)^2},\]
from which the phase of the current relative to the voltage is
\[\tan\phi = \frac{1 - \dfrac{\omega^2}{\omega_0^2}}{\omega\tau}.\]
The physical current is therefore
\[I(t) = |\tilde{I}|\cos(\omega t + \phi).\]

% ------- 8.5 Quality Factor -------
\subsection{Quality Factor}

The \textbf{quality factor} $Q$ measures the sharpness of the resonance peak:
\[Q = \frac{1}{\omega_0\tau} = \frac{\omega_0 L}{R}.\]
A high $Q$ corresponds to a narrow resonance. As $R \to 0$, the circuit approaches a pure LC resonator, $Q \to \infty$, and only oscillation at $\omega_0$ can be sustained. At resonance the capacitive and inductive reactances are equal in magnitude but opposite in phase (offset by $\pi$ radians), so they cancel exactly and the circuit presents purely resistive impedance to the driving source.

% ------- 8.6 Power and Resonance -------
\subsection{Power and Resonance}

The instantaneous power delivered to the circuit is
\[P(t) = V(t)\,I(t) = V_0 I_0\cos(\omega t)\cos(\omega t + \phi).\]
Time-averaging over one complete cycle gives
\[\langle P \rangle = \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T P(t)\,dt = \frac{V_0 I_0}{2}\cos\phi.\]
The factor $\cos\phi$ is called the \textbf{power factor}. Capacitors and inductors introduce a phase offset $\phi$ that reduces the efficiency of power delivery. In a pure LC circuit the phase offset is necessarily $\pm\pi/2$, so $\cos\phi = 0$ and the time-averaged power input vanishes --- no net energy is delivered unless the circuit is driven exactly at resonance $\omega = \omega_0$, where the inductive and capacitive impedances cancel and $\phi = 0$.

% ------- 8.7 Capacitor Voltage in a Driven RLC Circuit -------
\subsection{Capacitor Voltage in a Driven RLC Circuit}
\marginpar{March 10, 2026}

For the driven series RLC circuit of Section~8.4, the voltage drop across the capacitor is
\[\Delta V_C = \frac{Q}{C} = \frac{1}{C}\int_0^t I(t')\,dt'.\]
Using the complex current $\tilde{I} = |\tilde{I}|e^{j(\omega t + \phi)}$, integrating gives
\[\tilde{V}_C = \frac{\tilde{I}}{j\omega C} = \frac{V_0}{\sqrt{\left(1 - \dfrac{\omega^2}{\omega_0^2}\right)^2 + (\omega\tau)^2}}\,e^{j(\omega t + \phi - \pi/2)}.\]
Equivalently, from the impedance divider,
\[\frac{\tilde{V}'}{\tilde{V}} = \frac{Z_C}{Z_{\text{tot}}} = \frac{1}{1 - \dfrac{\omega^2}{\omega_0^2} + j\omega\tau},\]
with amplitude and phase
\[\left|\frac{\tilde{V}'}{\tilde{V}}\right|^2 = \frac{1}{\left(1 - \dfrac{\omega^2}{\omega_0^2}\right)^2 + (\omega\tau)^2}, \qquad \phi = -\arctan\!\left(\frac{\omega\tau}{1 - \dfrac{\omega^2}{\omega_0^2}}\right).\]

The capacitor voltage peaks near, but slightly below, $\omega_0$, because the peak is determined by the combination of maximum current flow and the frequency-dependent capacitive reactance, rather than by maximising the current alone.

\subsubsection{Limiting Behaviour}

\paragraph{Low frequency ($\omega \ll \omega_0$).}
The capacitive impedance dominates and nearly all of the input voltage is dropped across the capacitor. The output amplitude approaches $V_0$ and the phase offset approaches zero.

\paragraph{At resonance ($\omega = \omega_0$).}
Current is limited only by $R$ and reaches its maximum. Large voltage drops appear across both the inductor and capacitor, but they cancel. The current is in phase with $V(t)$; since the capacitor voltage lags the current by $\pi/2$, it lags the input by $\pi/2$.

\paragraph{High frequency ($\omega \gg \omega_0$).}
The voltage drops almost entirely across the inductor, so $V(t) \approx L\frac{dI}{dt}$, giving
\[I(t) \approx \frac{1}{L}\int V(t')\,dt' = \frac{V_0}{\omega L}\sin(\omega t).\]
The capacitor voltage is then
\[\Delta V_C = \frac{1}{C}\int I(t')\,dt' \approx -\frac{V_0}{\omega^2 LC}\cos(\omega t) = -\left(\frac{\omega_0}{\omega}\right)^2 V(t).\]
The inductor current lags $V(t)$ by $\pi/2$; the capacitor voltage lags the current by a further $\pi/2$, so the capacitor voltage lags $V(t)$ by $\pi$ in total.

\begin{remark}
    The \textbf{natural frequency} $\omega_0$ is the inherent rate at which energy oscillates between the inductor and capacitor in the absence of external driving. The \textbf{resonant frequency} is the specific driving frequency that produces maximum current. For low damping these are nearly identical; for large resistance they diverge.
\end{remark}

% ------- 8.8 Quality Factor -------
\subsection{Quality Factor}

The quality factor $Q_f$ is a dimensionless measure of the ratio of energy stored to energy dissipated per cycle:
\[Q_f = 2\pi\,\frac{W_s}{W_L},\]
where $W_s$ is the energy stored in the circuit and $W_L$ is the energy lost as heat per cycle.

\subsubsection{Derivation for a Series RLC Circuit}
Taking $I(t) = I_0\cos(\omega_0 t)$, the maximum energy stored (in the inductor at peak current) is
\[W_s = \tfrac{1}{2}LI_0^2.\]
By Joule's law\footnote{Joule's heating law states that the heat produced in a conductor is proportional to the square of the current, the resistance, and the duration of flow.}, the energy dissipated per cycle $T_0 = 2\pi/\omega_0$ is
\[W_L = R\int_0^{T_0} I_0^2\cos^2(\omega_0 t)\,dt = \frac{RI_0^2 T_0}{2} = \frac{\pi R I_0^2}{\omega_0}.\]
Therefore,
\[Q_f = 2\pi\cdot\frac{\tfrac{1}{2}LI_0^2}{\dfrac{\pi R I_0^2}{\omega_0}} = \frac{\omega_0 L}{R} = \frac{1}{\omega_0\tau}.\]

% ------- 8.9 Quality Factor and Energy Decay -------
\subsection{Quality Factor and Energy Decay}

The quality factor is also directly related to the rate at which energy drains from a freely discharging RLC circuit. The current amplitude decays as
\[I_0(t) = I_0(0)\,e^{-\Gamma t/2}, \qquad \Gamma = \frac{R}{L},\]
so the stored energy decays as
\[E(t) = \tfrac{1}{2}LI_0^2(t) = E_0\,e^{-\Gamma t} \implies \frac{dE}{dt} = -\Gamma E.\]
The number of radians of oscillation completed per energy $e$-folding time is
\[Q_f = \omega_0\,\frac{E}{|dE/dt|} = \frac{\omega_0}{\Gamma} = \frac{\omega_0 L}{R} = \frac{1}{\omega_0\tau},\]
consistent with the result above. A high $Q_f$ therefore means the circuit loses a small fraction of its stored energy per oscillation cycle.

\subsubsection*{AC Circuits}

Complex current:
\[I = I_0\,e^{j(\omega t+\phi_0)} = \tilde{I}\,e^{j\omega t}.\]

Impedances:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Resistor: $Z_R = R$
    \item Capacitor: $Z_C = \dfrac{1}{j\omega C}$
    \item Inductor: $Z_L = j\omega L$
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Miscellaneous}

\textit{Transient}: The short initial response when a circuit is switched on, typically involving terms like $e^{-t/\tau}$.

\textit{Steady-state}: The long-term behaviour of the circuit after transients vanish.

Filtering rules:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{Low-pass filter}: Blocks high-frequency signals; allows low-frequency signals to pass. Often an RC circuit with output across the capacitor, or an LR circuit where the inductor appears first in the signal path.
    \item \textit{High-pass filter}: Blocks low-frequency/DC signals; allows high-frequency signals to pass. Commonly an RC circuit with output across the resistor, or an LR circuit where the resistor precedes the inductor.
\end{itemize}

Water analogy:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Resistor $\leftrightarrow$ friction; dissipates energy.
    \item Inductor $\leftrightarrow$ inertia; resists changes in current.
    \item Capacitor $\leftrightarrow$ spring; stores electrical energy.
\end{itemize}

Phase shifts (ICE vs ELI):
\begin{itemize}
    \item In a capacitor, current leads voltage (ICE).
    \item In an inductor, voltage leads current (ELI).
\end{itemize}

Damping cases in an RLC circuit:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{Underdamped}: Oscillatory behaviour before settling.
    \item \textit{Overdamped}: No oscillations; system slowly approaches equilibrium.
    \item \textit{Critically damped}: Fastest return to equilibrium without oscillations.
\end{itemize}

\textit{Integrator}: Circuit where the output is proportional to the integral of the input; associated with $\frac{1}{j\omega}$.

\textit{Differentiator}: Circuit where the output corresponds to the rate of change of the input; associated with $j\omega$.

\textit{Complex impedance $Z$}: Treat impedance like resistance when solving AC circuits using $V = IZ$.

Decibels (dB) for Bode plots:
\[\text{Gain (dB)} = 20\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{V_{\text{out}}}{V_{\text{in}}}\right).\]

$-3\,\text{dB}$ point: The cutoff where the signal power is reduced by half.

Cutoff frequency $\omega_c$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item RC circuit: $\omega_c = \dfrac{1}{RC}$
    \item LR circuit: $\omega_c = \dfrac{R}{L}$
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Tip}: For sinusoidal inputs, replace $L$ and $C$ with their impedance expressions and analyze the circuit using standard algebra instead of solving differential equations.
