\subsection{Waves}

\subsubsection{Properties of Waves}
To describe waves, considering a sinusoidal wave of the form \(A_p\sin(2\pi\nu t)\), we use the following terms:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Peak amplitude (\(A_p\)): maximum value of the wave from its equilibrium position.
    \item Peak-to-peak amplitude: total height of the wave from its maximum to its minimum value (i.e., \(2A_p\)).
    \item Frequency (\(\nu\)): number of cycles per second (Hz).
    \item Time (\(t\)): time variable.
\end{itemize}
To describe AC signals effectively, we take the root mean square (RMS) amplitude \(\frac{A_p}{\sqrt{2}}\) of values such as current and voltage. This is because the mean value of an AC signal is zero, and the RMS is a more meaningful measure of the signal's amplitude as it gives a positive value that is proportional to the energy in the signal.

\subsubsection{Waveforms}
\begin{definition}[Waveform]
    A waveform is a graphical representation of how a signal varies over time, and can take various forms such as a sine wave, square wave, triangle wave, or sawtooth wave.
\end{definition}

% ------- 3.1 Electric Charge -------
\subsection{Electric Charge}
\begin{definition}[Electric Charge]
    Charge is a fundamental physical property that comes in two types: positive (+) and negative (--) (which cancel). Opposite charges attract and equal amounts of positive and negative charge cancel, so most matter is electrically neutral.
\end{definition}
\begin{proposition}[Conservation of Charge]
    Electric charge is conserved, meaning it cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proposition}[Quantization of Charge]
    Electric charge is also quantized, existing in discrete multiples of the elementary charge
    \[e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C},\]
    where the coulomb (C) is the SI unit of charge.
\end{proposition}

% ------- 3.2 Electric Field -------
\subsection{Electric Field}
\begin{definition}[Electric Field]
    The electric field $\vec{E}$ is a vector field produced by electric charges. It describes the force that a charge would experience at any point in space. Quantitatively, the electric field at a point is defined as the force $\vec{F}$ acting on a small positive test charge $q$ placed at that point, divided by the magnitude of the charge:
    \[\vec{E} = \frac{\vec{F}}{q}.\]
\end{definition}
\begin{proposition}[Direction of Electric Field]
    The direction of $\vec{E}$ is the direction of the force on a positive test charge.
\end{proposition}
\begin{remark}
    The SI units for an electric field are newtons per coulomb (N/C), which are equivalent to volts per meter (V/m).
\end{remark}

% ------- 3.3 Electric Potential -------
\subsection{Electric Potential}
\begin{definition}[Electric Potential]
    The electric potential is a scalar quantity with units of volts (V) that represents the work done per unit charge to move a positive test charge from a reference point (often taken at infinity) to the point $\vec{r}$, without changing its kinetic energy.   
\end{definition}
\begin{proposition}[Formula for Electric Potential]
    The electric potential $V(\vec{r})$ at a point is the electric potential energy per unit charge:
    \[V = \frac{U}{q}.\]
\end{proposition}
\begin{proposition}[Relation to Electric Field]
    It is also defined relative to a reference point $\vec{r}_0$ by
    \[V(\vec{r}) - V(\vec{r}_0) = -\int_{\vec{r}_0}^{\vec{r}} \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l},\]
    where $\vec{E}$ is the electric field. The electric field is related to the potential by
    \[\vec{E} = -\nabla V.\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{example}[Uniform Electric Field]
    If $\vec{E}$ is uniform, the potential difference between two points separated by $\vec{d}$ is
    \[\Delta V = -\vec{E} \cdot \vec{d}.\]
\end{example}

% ------- 3.4 Electric Potential Energy -------
\subsection{Electric Potential Energy}
\begin{definition}[Electric Potential Energy]
    Electric potential energy $U$ is the energy a charge possesses due to its position in an electric field. Since electric potential is defined relative to a reference point, the potential energy is also defined relative to that same reference.
\end{definition}
\begin{proposition}[Formula for Electric Potential Energy]
    For a charge $q$ at a point where the electric potential is $V$, the potential energy is
    \[U = qV,\]
    and is measured in joules (J).
\end{proposition}
An electron placed between two charged plates experiences a potential difference $\Delta V$. The change in its electric potential energy is
\[\Delta U = q\,\Delta V.\]
If the electron is allowed to move freely, this change in electric potential energy is converted into kinetic energy:
\[\Delta K = -\Delta U.\]

% ------- 3.5 Magnetic Field -------
\subsection{Magnetic Field}
\begin{definition}[Magnetic Field]
    A magnetic field $\vec{B}$ is a vector field produced by moving electric charges (currents) and intrinsic magnetic moments of particles. It exerts a force on moving charges given by the Lorentz force law, which is perpendicular to both the velocity and the magnetic field.
\end{definition}
\begin{remark}[Magnetic Fields vs. Electric Fields]
    Unlike electric fields, which act on charges whether stationary or moving, magnetic fields act only on moving charges.
\end{remark}
\begin{theorem}[Lorentz Force Law]
    The force $\vec{F}$ on a charge $q$ moving with velocity $\vec{v}$ in a magnetic field $\vec{B}$ is given by
    \[\vec{F} = q(\vec{v} \times \vec{B}).\]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proposition}[Magnetic Field from a Current-Carrying Wire]
    For a long straight current-carrying wire, the magnitude of the magnetic field at a distance $r$ is
    \[B = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r},\]
    where $\mu_0$ is the permeability of free space\footnote{The permeability of free space is $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\,\mathrm{T} \cdot \mathrm{m/A}$.} and $I$ is the current. The SI unit of magnetic field is the tesla (T).
\end{proposition}

% ------- 3.6 Magnetic Flux -------
\subsection{Magnetic Flux}
\begin{definition}[Magnetic Flux]
    Magnetic flux $\Phi_B$ through a surface is defined as the surface integral of the magnetic field $\vec{B}$ over that surface:
    \[\Phi_B = \int \vec{B} \cdot d\vec{A},\]
    where $d\vec{A}$ is an infinitesimal area element of the surface, and the dot product accounts for the angle between the magnetic field and the normal to the surface.
\end{definition}
\begin{remark}
    The SI unit of magnetic flux is the weber (Wb).
\end{remark}

% ------- 3.7 Electromagnetic Induction -------
\marginpar{January 22, 2026}
\subsection{Electromagnetic Induction}

\subsubsection{Faraday's Law}
\begin{theorem}[Faraday's Law]
    Faraday's Law states that a changing magnetic flux through a closed loop induces an electromotive force (EMF) in the loop:
    \[\mathcal{E}_{\text{EMF}} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt},\]
    where $\mathcal{E}_{\text{EMF}}$ is the induced EMF and $\Phi_B$ is the magnetic flux through a wire loop. The negative sign indicates that the induced EMF creates a current whose magnetic field opposes the change in flux, as described by Lenz's Law.
\end{theorem}

\subsubsection{Lenz's Law}
\begin{law}[Lenz's Law]
    The induced current in a circuit flows in a direction such that the magnetic field it produces opposes the change in magnetic flux that generated it.
\end{law}
Consider any closed conducting loop. If the magnetic flux through the loop increases, the induced current produces a magnetic field that opposes the increase. If the flux decreases, the induced current produces a field that tends to restore it.

When the magnetic flux is increasing, the induced current is directed opposite to the orientation predicted by the right-hand rule for the increasing field. When the flux is decreasing, the induced current follows the orientation that attempts to reinforce the original field.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[scale=0.5]{../Images/Lenzslaw.png}
    \caption{Direction of the induced current according to Lenz's Law.}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Mutual Inductance}
\begin{definition}[Mutual Inductance]
    Mutual inductance is the property of two nearby circuits whereby a time-varying current in one coil induces an electromotive force (EMF) in another through magnetic flux linkage.
\end{definition}
If a current $I_1(t)$ flows in the primary coil, it produces a magnetic flux $\Phi_{21}$ through the secondary coil. According to Faraday's law,
\[\mathcal{E}_2 = -\frac{d\Phi_{21}}{dt}.\]
The mutual inductance $M$ is defined by
\[\Phi_{21} = M I_1,\]
which gives
\[\mathcal{E}_2 = -M \frac{dI_1}{dt}.\]
The value of $M$ depends on the number of turns in each coil, their geometry, their separation, and the magnetic permeability of the material between them.

\section{Circuit Fundamentals}

% ------- 4.1 Current and Voltage -------
\subsection{Current and Voltage}

\begin{definition}[Electric Current]
    Electric current is the flow of electrons through a wire driven by an electric field potential energy difference.
\end{definition}
\begin{proposition}
    The current is defined as the rate of charge past a point in a circuit:
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:current}
        I = \frac{dQ}{dt},
    \end{equation}
    and is measured in amperes (A), where $1\,\text{A} = 1\,\text{C/s}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{definition}[Voltage]
    The voltage (or electric potential difference) is the difference in potential energy between two points in an electric field.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Electron-volt]
    An electron-volt (eV) is a unit of energy equal to the energy gained or lost by an electron when it moves through an electric potential difference of one volt.
\end{definition}
\begin{proposition}
    Since energy is given by $U = qV$, for a charge equal to the elementary charge $e$, we have
    \[1\,\text{eV} = e \cdot 1\,\text{V} = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{J}.\]
\end{proposition}

When analyzing circuits, current is defined using the convention of positive charge flow. By convention, current flows from higher potential to lower potential, even though in metallic conductors the actual charge carriers are electrons moving in the opposite direction.

When the circuit is closed, electrons flow externally from the anode to the cathode, while conventional current flows from the cathode to the anode. As the charges move through the circuit, the battery's chemical energy is converted into electrical energy and then into other forms such as heat or light.

% ------- 4.2 Electromotive Force -------
\marginpar{January 15, 2026}
\subsection{Electromotive Force}

\begin{definition}[Electromotive Force (EMF)]
    Electromotive Force (EMF)\footnote{EMF is the total energy supplied per unit charge by a source. The terminal voltage across a real source is typically less than the EMF due to internal resistance.} is the energy supplied per unit charge by an energy source such as a battery or generator. It represents the work done by the source to move a charge around a complete circuit. EMF is measured in volts (V).
\end{definition}

To better understand the concept of EMF, consider the following analogy: EMF is like a voltage credit that is then all spent by dropping it over the elements of the circuit. Think of your checking account with two columns: credit (money in) and debit (money spent). EMF is the ``credit'' that is then all ``spent'' by dropping it over the elements of the circuit.

% ------- 4.3 Ohm's Law -------
\subsection{Ohm's Law}

\begin{definition}[Ohm's Law]
    Ohm's Law states that the current \(I\) flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to the voltage \(V\) across it and inversely proportional to its resistance \(R\):
    \[I = \frac{V}{R}.\]
\end{definition}

Microscopically, Ohm's Law can be understood in terms of the motion of electrons in a conductor. When a voltage is applied across a conductor, it creates an electric field that exerts a force on the free electrons, causing them to move and create an electric current. The resistance of the conductor arises from collisions between the electrons and the atoms in the material, which impede their motion.

The current density $J$ is related to the electric field strength $E$ by
\[J = \sigma E, \qquad E = J\rho_r,\]
where $\sigma$ is the conductivity and $\rho_r$ is the resistivity. The current is
\[I = AJ = A\sigma E.\]
Using $E = \Delta V/l$,
\[I = \frac{A\sigma\,\Delta V}{l} = \frac{A\,\Delta V}{\rho_r l}.\]
From Ohm's Law, $\Delta V = IR$, which gives the resistance in terms of material properties:
\[R = \frac{\rho_r l}{A}.\]

Ohm's Law can be represented graphically using the Ohm's Law Wheel, which illustrates the relationships between current, voltage, resistance, and conductance.

\end{figure}

% ------- 4.4 Kirchhoff's Laws -------
\subsection{Kirchhoff's Laws}

\begin{definition}[Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL)]
    The algebraic sum of all potential differences around any closed loop in a circuit is equal to zero.
\end{definition}

KVL allows us to determine the potential difference between two points in a circuit by accounting for the voltage changes across each component along the path.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{../Images/kirchhoffrule.jpg}
    \caption{Each resistor and voltage source is traversed from $a$ to $b$. The corresponding potential changes across each element are indicated. (The script $\mathcal{E}$ denotes the emf.)}
    \label{klawformula}
\end{figure}

Another common circuit element is the inductor. In the context of KVL, the voltage across an inductor is included in the loop equation in the same way as the voltage across resistors or capacitors.

\begin{definition}[Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL)]
    The sum of the currents entering a node is equal to the sum of the currents leaving the node.
\end{definition}

% ------- 4.7 Circuit Classification -------
\subsection{Circuit Classification}

Circuits may be classified according to whether they are linear or non-linear, and whether they contain active or passive elements. A linear circuit element has a linear (straight-line) relationship between voltage and current, meaning $V \propto I$; a non-linear element does not.

\begin{definition}[Passive Linear Circuit]
    Contains only passive linear elements, such as resistors, capacitors, inductors, and transformers.
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Active Linear Circuit]
    Contains at least one active element (e.g.\ transistor, op-amp, or independent source) while maintaining an overall linear $V$--$I$ relationship.
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Passive Non-linear Circuit]
    Contains only passive components but includes at least one non-linear element, such as a diode.
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Active Non-linear Circuit]
    Contains at least one active element and at least one non-linear element, such as a transistor with a non-linear $V$--$I$ characteristic like a diode.
\end{definition}

% ------- 5.1 Resistors -------
\subsection{Resistors}

\begin{definition}
    A resistor is a device that opposes the flow of current through it, resulting in a voltage drop across it. Resistors are characterized by their resistance value \(R\), measured in ohms (\(\Omega\)).
\end{definition}
\begin{proposition}[Series Resistors]
    When resistors are connected in series, their resistances add: $R_{\text{tot}} = R_1 + R_2 + \cdots + R_n$. 
\end{proposition}
\begin{proposition}[Parallel Resistors]
    When resistors are in parallel, they add inversely: $\frac{1}{R_{\text{tot}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \cdots + \frac{1}{R_n}$.
\end{proposition}

\subsubsection{Temperature Dependence}
The resistance of many metals varies with temperature as
\[R_T = R_0\left[1 + \alpha(T - T_0)\right],\]
where \(R_0\) is the resistance at reference temperature \(T_0\) and \(\alpha\) is the linear temperature coefficient.

\subsubsection{Power Dissipation}
When current flows through a resistor, electrical energy is converted into heat. The power \(P\) dissipated is
\[P = \Delta V \cdot I = I^2 R = \frac{(\Delta V)^2}{R}.\]
The total energy dissipated over time is
\[U = \int R\,I^2(t)\,dt.\]

% ------- 5.2 Batteries -------
\subsection{Batteries}

\begin{definition}[Battery]
    A battery converts stored chemical energy into electrical energy by maintaining a potential difference between its terminals. Inside the battery, chemical reactions separate charge, creating an excess of negative charge at the anode and positive charge at the cathode.
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Ideal Battery]
    An ideal battery is a voltage source with zero internal resistance that maintains a constant voltage regardless of the current drawn.
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Real Battery]
    A real battery has internal resistance $r$ that causes the terminal voltage to decrease as the current increases, leading to energy loss as heat.
\end{definition}

A real battery is modelled as an ideal voltage source \(V_0\) in series with an internal resistance \(r\). The terminal voltage when current \(I\) is drawn is
\[V_{\text{terminal}} = V_0 - Ir.\]
If an external resistor $R$ is connected, Ohm's Law gives
\[V_0 = Ir + IR.\]

% ------- 5.3 Ground and Reference -------
\subsection{Ground and Reference}

\begin{definition}[Circuit Ground]
    The reference point in a circuit at which the electric potential is defined to be zero. All voltages in the circuit are measured relative to this point. It does not have to be physically connected to the Earth. Symbol: \begin{circuitikz}\draw (0,0) node[ground]{};\end{circuitikz}.
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Chassis Ground]
    A conductive frame or enclosure of a device used as a common return path for current; often connected to circuit ground. It provides shielding and safety by preventing exposed metal parts from reaching dangerous voltages. Symbol: \begin{circuitikz}\draw (0,0) node[sground]{};\end{circuitikz}.
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Earth Ground]
    A physical connection to the Earth, typically made using a metal rod or pipe driven into the soil, used primarily for safety to allow fault currents to safely dissipate.
\end{definition}

% ------- 5.4 Capacitors -------
\subsection{Capacitors}

\begin{definition}[Capacitor]
    A capacitor is a passive electrical component that stores energy in an electric field between two conductive plates separated by an insulating material (dielectric) or space.
\end{definition}
\begin{proposition}[Capacitance]
    The capacitance \(C\) is defined as the ratio of the charge \(Q\) stored on one plate to the voltage \(V\) across the plates:
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eq:capacitance}
        C = \frac{Q}{V}.
    \end{equation}
    The SI unit of capacitance is the farad (F).
\end{proposition}
For a parallel plate capacitor,
\[C = \frac{\varepsilon A}{d},\]
where \(\varepsilon\) is the dielectric constant, \(A\) is the area of the plates, and \(d\) is the distance between them. The permittivity of free space is
\[\varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12}\,\text{F/m}.\]
\begin{proposition}[Series Capacitors]
    When capacitors are connected in series, they add inversely: $\frac{1}{C_{\text{tot}}} = \frac{1}{C_1} + \frac{1}{C_2} + \ldots + \frac{1}{C_n}$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proposition}[Parallel Capacitors]
    When capacitors are connected in parallel, their capacitances add: $C_{\text{tot}} = C_1 + C_2 + \ldots + C_n$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{remark}
    Capacitors combine oppositely to resistors. This is because the voltage across capacitors in series is divided among them, while the charge on capacitors in parallel is shared.
\end{remark}

\subsubsection{Dielectric Insulator}
\begin{definition}[Dielectric Insulator]
    A dielectric is an insulating material that permits an electric field but does not conduct charge. When placed between capacitor plates, it increases capacitance by reducing the effective electric field, allowing more charge to be stored for a given voltage. Its permittivity is
    \[\varepsilon = \varepsilon_r\,\varepsilon_0,\]
    where $\varepsilon_r$ is the relative permittivity.
\end{definition}

\subsubsection{Energy Stored in a Capacitor}
The energy stored equals the work required to move charge from one plate to the other. Since $V = Q/C$,
\[W = \int_0^Q V\,dQ = \int_0^Q \frac{Q}{C}\,dQ = \frac{1}{2}CV^2.\]

\subsubsection{Charging a Capacitor}
From \eqref{eq:capacitance} and \eqref{eq:current}, the current-voltage relationship for a capacitor is
\[I = C\frac{dV}{dt}, \qquad V(t) = \frac{1}{C}\int I\,dt + V_0.\]
For constant current,
\[V(t) = V_0 + \frac{I}{C}\,t,\]
so the voltage increases linearly with slope $I/C$.
\begin{center}

\subsection{Inductors}

\begin{definition}[Inductor]
    An inductor is a passive electrical component that stores energy in a magnetic field created by the flow of current through a coil of wire. The inductance \(L\) of an inductor is defined as the ratio of the magnetic flux \(\Phi\) linking the coil to the current \(I\) flowing through it:
    \[L = \frac{\Phi}{I}.\]
    The SI unit of inductance is the henry (H).
\end{definition}

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.3\textwidth]{../Images/Inductor_symbols.png}
    \caption{Circuit symbols for inductors with different core types.}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection{Energy Storage}
Energy is stored in the magnetic field within the inductor coil. For a constant applied voltage, the current in the coil rises linearly with slope $V/L$:
\begin{center}
    \begin{tikzpicture}
        \draw[->] (0,0) -- (5,0) node[right] {$t$};
        \draw[->] (0,0) -- (0,4) node[above] {$I$};

        \draw (0,1) -- (4,3);
        \node at (4,2.6) {$\frac{V}{L}$};
    \end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}
The energy stored is
\[W = \int_0^t V\cdot i\,dt = \int L\frac{di}{dt}\cdot i\,dt = \frac{1}{2}LI^2.\]

\subsubsection{Networks of Inductors}

\paragraph{Inductors in Series.}
The equivalent inductance is
\[L_{\text{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} L_i.\]

\paragraph{Inductors in Parallel.}
The equivalent inductance satisfies
\[\frac{1}{L_{\text{total}}} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{1}{L_i}.\]

These relations are formally identical to those of resistors, but arise from the inductor voltage-current relation.

\subsubsection{Solenoid}
\begin{definition}[Solenoid]
    A solenoid is a long cylindrical coil of wire designed to produce a nearly uniform magnetic field inside it when current flows.
\end{definition}
For a solenoid of length $l$, cross-sectional area $A = \pi r^2$, number of turns $N$, and core permeability $\mu$,
\[L = \frac{\mu N^2 A}{l}.\]

\subsection{Transformers \& Mutual Inductance (Not Tested)}

\begin{definition}[Transformer]
    A transformer is a device consisting of two coils of wire (primary and secondary) magnetically coupled through a shared core. Its main purpose is to transfer energy between circuits to change voltage levels. Symbol:
    \begin{center}
        \begin{circuitikz}
            \draw (0,0) node[transformer] (T) {}
            (T.A1) -- ++(-1,0)
            (T.A2) -- ++(-1,0)
            (T.B1) -- ++(1,0)
            (T.B2) -- ++(1,0);
        \end{circuitikz}
    \end{center}
\end{definition}

A transformer has $N_1$ and $N_2$ turns wound around a common core. A time-varying current $I_1$ in the primary produces flux $\Phi_B$ through the secondary. By Faraday's law,
\[\mathcal{E}_2 = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = -M\frac{dI_1}{dt}.\]

\begin{center}
    \begin{figure}[H]
        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{../Images/Transformer.png}
        \caption{Visual representation of a transformer.}
    \end{figure}
\end{center}

The SI unit of mutual inductance is the henry (H). If $I_1(t) = I_0\sin(\omega t)$, then
\[V_2(t) = M\omega I_0\cos(\omega t),\]
so the secondary voltage is sinusoidal but shifted in phase by $\pi/2$ relative to the current.

For an ideal transformer, power is conserved ($V_1 I_1 = V_2 I_2$), giving the transformer relations:
\[\frac{V_1}{V_2} = \frac{N_1}{N_2}, \qquad \frac{I_1}{I_2} = \frac{N_2}{N_1}.\]

\begin{remark}
    Long-distance power lines use high-voltage AC because it significantly reduces energy loss during transmission: higher voltage means lower current, which minimises resistive heat loss. Transformers efficiently step AC voltage up and down as needed.
\end{remark}

% ------- 6.2 RC Circuit with AC Driving -------
\subsubsection{AC Analysis}
\marginpar{January 29, 2026}
Let an RC circuit be driven by a periodic input with period $T$ and an angular frequency $\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T} = 2\pi\nu$.
\begin{definition}[Time Constant]
    The time constant for an RC circuit is
    \[\tau_{RC} = RC.\]
\end{definition}
\begin{proposition}[Frequency Response]
    The circuit behaviour depends on the ratio of $T$ to $\tau$:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Low frequency: $T \gg \tau$
        \item High frequency: $T \ll \tau$
    \end{itemize}
\end{proposition}

If the input voltage is a square wave, the circuit response will differ significantly between low and high frequencies.
\begin{proposition}[Low Frequency]
    Consider a low-frequency ($T \gg \tau$) square-wave input, where the voltage alternates between $+V_0$ and $-V_0$ every half-cycle. The capacitor has enough time to fully charge and discharge during each half-cycle, resulting in the following behaviour:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Capacitor fully charges and discharges each half-cycle.
        \item Voltage drops almost entirely across the capacitor.
        \item Current is nearly zero (except for spikes at each voltage jump).
        \item Capacitor behaves approximately like an open circuit (break).
    \end{itemize}
\end{proposition}
At low frequency, the capacitor has time to fully charge, so its voltage tracks the input: $\Delta V_C(t) \approx V(t)$. The current is therefore $I \approx C\frac{dV}{dt}$, and the voltage across the resistor is
\[\Delta V_R(t) = R\frac{dQ}{dt} \approx \tau\frac{dV_{\text{in}}(t)}{dt}, \qquad \tau = RC.\]
The resistor acts as a differentiator. Sharp transitions in the square-wave input produce current spikes.
\begin{proposition}[High Frequency]
    For a high-frequency ($T \ll \tau$) square-wave input, the capacitor does not have time to charge significantly during each half-cycle, leading to the following behaviour:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Capacitor does not have time to charge.
        \item Very little voltage is dropped across the capacitor.
        \item Current values are close to what we expect for a pure resistor circuit; voltage profile is triangle-wave-like.
        \item Capacitor behaves approximately like a short circuit.
    \end{itemize}
\end{proposition}
At high frequency, the capacitor has no time to charge, so the resistor voltage tracks the input: $\Delta V_R(t) \approx V(t)$, giving $I \approx V(t)/R$. The capacitor voltage is then
\[\Delta V_C(t) = \frac{Q}{C} \approx \frac{1}{\tau}\int_0^t V_{\text{in}}(t')\,dt', \qquad \tau = RC.\]
The capacitor acts as an integrator. A square wave input produces a triangle-wave-like voltage across the capacitor.

\subsection{LR Circuit}
\begin{definition}[LR Circuit]
    An LR circuit is a circuit in which an inductor is connected in series with a resistor.
\end{definition}
\begin{example}[LR Circuit]
    Consider this circuit at the moment it is turned on (switch connects to `A'). If the inductor were replaced with a short, a current $V/R$ would flow immediately, with all of the voltage drop across $R$.
    \begin{figure}[H]
        \begin{center}
            \centering
            \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{../Images/LR_circuit.png}
            \caption{Visual representation of an LR circuit.}
            \label{LR-circuit}
        \end{center}
    \end{figure}
    The current flow produces a magnetic flux through the coil. By Lenz's law, the coil induces a voltage equal to the battery's voltage, opposing the change in flux. Therefore, just at the instant the switch is closed, the current flow is zero.
\end{example}
For a single coil, the voltage-current relationship is
\[V = L\frac{di}{dt},\]
where $L$ is the self-inductance.

\subsubsection{DC Analysis}
\subsubsection*{Charging}
When the switch is connected to terminal A,
\[I(t) = \frac{V}{R}\!\left(1 - e^{-t/\tau}\right), \qquad V_L(t) = V\,e^{-t/\tau}.\]
At $t = 0$, an inductor behaves like an open circuit. As $t \to \infty$, it behaves like a short circuit and carries the full steady-state current $V/R$.

\subsubsection*{Discharging}
\[V_L(t) = V_0\,e^{-t/\tau}, \qquad I(t) = I_0\,e^{-t/\tau}.\]
Both current and voltage decay exponentially to zero.

% ------- 6.4 LR Circuit with AC Driving -------
\subsubsection{AC Analysis}
Let an LR circuit be driven by a periodic input with period $T$ and angular frequency $\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T} = 2\pi\nu$.
\begin{definition}[Time Constant]
    The time constant for an LR circuit is
    \[\tau_{LR} = \frac{L}{R}.\]
\end{definition}
\begin{proposition}[Frequency Response]
    The behavior of the circuit depends on the ratio of $T$ to $\tau$:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Low frequency: $T \gg \tau$
        \item High frequency: $T \ll \tau$
    \end{itemize}
\end{proposition}

If the input voltage is a square wave, the circuit response will differ significantly between low and high frequencies.
\begin{proposition}[Low Frequency]
    Consider a low-frequency ($T \gg \tau$) square-wave input, where the voltage alternates between $+V_0$ and $-V_0$ every half-cycle. The inductor has enough time to fully respond during each half-cycle, resulting in the following behaviour:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Inductor fully responds each half-cycle.
        \item Voltage drops almost entirely across the resistor.
        \item Current closely follows the input and is approximately square-wave-like.
        \item Inductor behaves approximately like a short circuit.
    \end{itemize}
\end{proposition}
At low frequency, the inductor has time to fully respond, so the resistor voltage tracks the input: $\Delta V_R(t) \approx V(t)$, giving $I \approx V_{\text{in}}(t)/R$. The voltage across the inductor is then
\[\Delta V_L(t) = L\frac{dI}{dt} \approx \tau\frac{dV_{\text{in}}(t)}{dt}, \qquad \tau = \frac{L}{R}.\]
The inductor acts as a differentiator. Sharp transitions in the square-wave input produce voltage spikes across the inductor.
\begin{proposition}[High Frequency]
    For a high-frequency ($T \ll \tau$) square-wave input, the inductor does not have time to respond during each half-cycle, leading to the following behaviour:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Inductor does not have time to respond.
        \item Voltage drops almost entirely across the inductor.
        \item Current changes very little; the resistor voltage profile consists of sharp spikes at each transition.
        \item Inductor behaves approximately like an open circuit.
    \end{itemize}
\end{proposition}
At high frequency, the inductor has no time to respond, so its voltage tracks the input: $\Delta V_L(t) \approx V(t)$, giving $L\frac{dI}{dt} \approx V_{\text{in}}(t)$. The voltage across the resistor is then
\[\Delta V_R(t) = IR \approx \frac{1}{\tau}\int_0^t V_{\text{in}}(t')\,dt', \qquad \tau = \frac{L}{R}.\]
The resistor acts as an integrator. A square-wave input produces a triangle-wave-like voltage across the resistor.

\section{Phasors and Complex Impedance}
\marginpar{February 5, 2026}

\subsection{Complex Representation of AC Signals}
\begin{definition}[Complex Signal]
    A complex signal is not physical, but it embeds the real-valued physical signal $V(t)$ as its real part. It is a mathematical tool that simplifies the analysis of AC circuits by allowing us to represent sinusoidal signals as complex exponentials, which are easier to manipulate using algebraic methods.
\end{definition}
\begin{proposition}[Complex Signal Representation]
    A complex signal is defined as
    \[\tilde{V}(t) = V_0 e^{j\phi(t)} = V_0 e^{j(\omega t + \phi_0)}.\]
\end{proposition}
\begin{proposition}[Physical Signal from Complex Signal]
    The real-valued physical signal $V(t)$ is the real part of the complex signal:
    \[V(t) = \operatorname{Re}\!\left[V_0 e^{j\phi(t)}\right] = \operatorname{Re}\!\left[\tilde{V}(t)\right].\]
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Working with Complex Signals}

To analyze an AC circuit using complex signals, perform circuit analysis\footnote{Circuit analysis is the process of determining the voltages and currents throughout a circuit by applying circuit laws (KVL, KCL) and component relationships. For AC circuits, this is done using the complex signal $\tilde{V}(t)$ in place of the real signal, which simplifies differentiation and integration into algebraic operations.} with $\tilde{V}(t)$ to obtain a complex output signal $\tilde{V}_{\text{out}}(t)$. The real-valued physical output is then recovered by taking the real part:
\[V_{\text{out}}(t) = \operatorname{Re}\!\left[\tilde{V}_{\text{out}}(t)\right].\]
Alternatively, one can extract directly the change in signal amplitude and phase shift between input and output.

\subsection{Capacitor Current}

Consider a voltage signal $V(t) = \operatorname{Re}[\tilde{V}(t)]$ dropped across a capacitor. To find the current $I(t)$, we first compute the complex current $\tilde{I}(t)$ arising from $\tilde{V}(t)$, then take the real part:
\[\tilde{I}(t) = \frac{d\tilde{Q}}{dt} = C\frac{d\tilde{V}(t)}{dt} = j C\omega V_0 e^{j(\omega t + \phi_0)} = j C\omega\tilde{V}.\]
Extracting $I(t)$ using the fact that $j = e^{j\pi/2}$:
\[I(t) = \operatorname{Re}\!\left[\tilde{I}(t)\right] = C\omega V_0\operatorname{Re}\!\left[e^{j(\omega t + \phi_0 + \pi/2)}\right] = C\omega V_0\cos\!\left(\omega t + \phi_0 + \tfrac{\pi}{2}\right).\]
\begin{proposition}
    From the expression above, the current leads the input voltage by $\pi/2$; equivalently, the input voltage lags the current by $\pi/2$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{remark}
    Note that $I$ vanishes in the limit $\omega \to 0$ (DC limit), since in steady state the capacitor fully charges and fully opposes the voltage source.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Impedance}
\marginpar{February 10, 2026}
\begin{definition}[Impedance]
    The impedance $Z$ of a circuit element is a complex resistance defined as the ratio of the complex voltage across the element to the complex current through it:
    \[Z = \frac{\tilde{V}}{\tilde{I}}.\]
    The real part of $Z$ governs energy dissipation; the imaginary part (the \textbf{reactance}) encodes the phase offset between voltage and current.
\end{definition}
\begin{proposition}[Impedance Combination]
    Impedances combine exactly as resistances do:
    \[Z_{\text{tot}} = Z_1 + Z_2 \quad \text{(series)}, \qquad Z_{\text{tot}} = \left(\frac{1}{Z_1} + \frac{1}{Z_2}\right)^{-1} \quad \text{(parallel)}.\]
\end{proposition}
\begin{definition}[Resistive Impedance]
    The impedance of a resistor is purely real:
    \[Z_R = R.\]
    Since $Z_R$ has no imaginary part, a resistor introduces no phase offset between voltage and current.
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Capacitive Impedance]
    The impedance of a capacitor is
    \[Z_C = \frac{1}{j\omega C}.\]
    Since $Z_C$ is purely imaginary and negative, the current leads the voltage by $\pi/2$. The magnitude $|Z_C| = 1/(\omega C)$ is large at low frequencies and falls off as $\omega$ increases; in the DC limit $\omega \to 0$, $Z_C \to \infty$, meaning the capacitor blocks DC signals entirely.
\end{definition}
\begin{remark}
    A capacitor causes current to lead voltage by $\pi/2$ because the current flowing into the capacitor must first accumulate charge on its plates before a voltage difference can be established. Since $I = C\frac{dV}{dt}$, maximum current flows when the voltage is changing fastest (crossing zero) and zero current flows when the voltage is stable at its peak.
\end{remark}
\begin{definition}[Inductive Impedance]
    The impedance of an inductor is
    \[Z_L = j\omega L.\]
    Since $Z_L$ is purely imaginary and positive, the current lags the voltage by $\pi/2$. The magnitude $|Z_L| = \omega L$ grows with frequency, so the inductor increasingly opposes high-frequency signals.
\end{definition}
\begin{remark}
    An inductor causes current to lag voltage because its magnetic properties, governed by Lenz's law, oppose changes in current flow ($V = L\frac{dI}{dt}$), causing the current waveform to reach its maximum $90°$ after the voltage waveform. Voltage is proportional to the rate of change of current, meaning maximum voltage occurs when current changes fastest (at zero crossing) and zero voltage occurs when the current is constant (at its peak), forcing a $90°$ phase shift.
\end{remark}
\begin{proposition}
    For any element with impedance $Z$, the complex current and voltage satisfy
    \[\tilde{I} = \frac{\tilde{V}}{Z},\]
    the direct complex generalisation of Ohm's Law.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Power Dissipation of AC Signals}
The power dissipated by a circuit element driven by an AC signal is found by averaging over one cycle:
\[P = \frac{1}{T}\int_{0}^{T} I(t)\,\Delta V(t)\,dt.\]
Taking $V(t) = V_0\cos(\omega t)$ and $I(t) = I_0\cos(\omega t + \phi_0)$, this gives
\[P = \frac{V_0 I_0}{T}\int_{0}^{T}\cos(\omega t)\cos(\omega t + \phi_0)\,dt = \frac{V_0 I_0}{2}\cos(\phi_0) = V_{\text{rms}}\,I_{\text{rms}}\cos(\phi_0).\]
For a capacitor and an inductor, $\phi_0 = \pm\pi/2$, so $P = 0$; they do not dissipate energy but instead store and release it. For a resistor, $\phi_0 = 0$, so $P = V_{\text{rms}}\,I_{\text{rms}}$; all the energy is dissipated as heat.


% ------- 7.9 Worked Example: Current in an RC Circuit -------
\subsection{Sinusoidally Driven RC Circuit}
\begin{example}[Current in Sinusoidally Driven RC Circuit]
    Let $\tilde{V}(t) = V_0 e^{j\omega t}$, $Z_R = R$, and $Z_C = \frac{1}{j\omega C}$, find the current in the following RC circuit:
    \begin{center}
        \begin{tikzpicture}[transform shape]
            \label{Circuit:ACRC}
            \draw (4, 3) to[sinusoidal voltage source] (4, 2.25);
            \draw (4.75, 4.25) to[american resistor] (7, 4.25);
            \draw (8, 4.25) to[capacitor] (8, 1.25);
            \node[sground] at (6, 1.25){};
            \draw (8, 1.25) |- (4, 1.25) -| (4, 2.25);
            \draw (7, 4.25) -- (8, 4.25);
            \draw (4.75, 4.25) |- (4, 4.25) -| (4, 3);
        \end{tikzpicture}
    \end{center}
\end{example}
First and foremost, we need to find the total impedance,
\[Z_{\text{tot}} = Z_R + Z_C = R + \frac{1}{j\omega C} = \frac{1 + j\omega RC}{j\omega C} = \frac{1+j\omega\tau}{j\omega C},\]
where $\tau = RC$.\\
After finding the total impedance, we are able to find the complex current,
\[\tilde{I}(t) = \frac{\tilde{V}(t)}{Z_{\text{tot}}} = \frac{j\omega C\, V_0\, e^{j\omega t}}{1 + j\omega\tau}.\]
The next step is to find the magnitude of the total impedance
\[|Z_{\text{tot}}|^2 = R^2 + \frac{1}{\omega^2 C^2} = R^2\,\frac{1 + (\omega\tau)^2}{(\omega\tau)^2},\]
for which we get the amplitude of the current
\[|\tilde{I}| = \frac{V_0}{|Z_{\text{tot}}|} = \frac{V_0}{R}\cdot\frac{\omega\tau}{\sqrt{1+(\omega\tau)^2}}.\]
\begin{remark}
    At $\omega = 0$ (DC limit) the current vanishes; as $\omega\to\infty$ it rises to $I_{\max} = V_0/R$, since the capacitive impedance vanishes.
\end{remark}
For the last step, we need to find the phase offset, since this is an RC circuit, the current leads the voltage due to the capacitor by
\[\phi = \arctan\!\left(\frac{1}{\omega\tau}\right).\]
The full complex current is therefore
\[\tilde{I}(t) = \frac{V_0}{R}\cdot\frac{\omega\tau}{\sqrt{1+(\omega\tau)^2}}\,e^{j(\omega t + \phi)}.\]
\begin{remark}[Limiting Cases]
    In the DC limit ($\omega\to 0$), the voltage drops almost entirely across the capacitor and the current leads the voltage by $\phi = \arctan(\infty) = \pi/2$. In the high-frequency limit ($\omega\to\infty$), the voltage drops almost entirely across the resistor and the current is in phase with the voltage: $\phi = \arctan(0) = 0$.
\end{remark}


\subsection{Transfer Function}
\marginpar{February 12, 2026}
Consider a general voltage divider circuit built from two impedances $Z_1$ and $Z_2$ in series, driven by a sinusoidal source. The output voltage $\tilde{V}'$ is taken across $Z_2$:
\begin{center}
    \begin{tikzpicture}[transform shape]
        \draw (3.5, 0) to[sinusoidal current source] (3.5, 1.75);

\section{RLC Circuits}

\subsection{Charging with a Step Voltage}
Consider the following series RLC circuit, with a switch closed at $t = 0$ to create a voltage step:
\begin{center}
    \begin{tikzpicture}[transform shape]
        \node[sground] at (-0.25, -1.5){};
        \draw (-2.25, -1.5) to[battery1] (-2.25, 0.5);
        \draw (-2.25, 0.5) -- (-3, 1.25);

        \draw (0.75, 2) to[cute inductor] (2.75, 2);
        \draw (2.75, 2) to[capacitor] (2.75, -1.5);
        \draw (-2.25, 1.5) -| (-2.25, 2) -- (-1.25, 2);
        \draw (-2.25, -1.5) -- (2.75, -1.5);
        \node[anchor=center] at (-2.85, -0.25){\scriptsize $V_0$};
    \end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}
Summing voltage drops around the loop (KVL) gives
\[V_0 = RI + L\frac{dI}{dt} + \frac{Q}{C}.\]
Differentiating with respect to time and dividing through by $L$,
\[\frac{d^2I}{dt^2} + \frac{R}{L}\frac{dI}{dt} + \frac{1}{LC}I = 0.\]
We define the natural resonant frequency
\[\omega_0 \equiv \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC}},\]
at which the inductive and capacitive reactances are equal in magnitude. At this frequency, energy oscillates efficiently between the inductor and capacitor, the circuit is purely resistive, and the impedance is minimised. We also define the damping factor
\[\Gamma \equiv \frac{R}{L},\]
which represents the rate at which energy is dissipated by the resistor. The equation of motion becomes
\[\frac{d^2I}{dt^2} + \Gamma\frac{dI}{dt} + \omega_0^2\,I = 0.\]

\subsection{Solution by Characteristic Equation}
Substituting the trial solution $I(t) = ce^{pt}$ gives the characteristic equation
\[p^2 + \Gamma p + \omega_0^2 = 0 \implies p = -\frac{\Gamma}{2} \pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{\Gamma}{2}\right)^2 - \omega_0^2}.\]
The nature of the solution depends on the sign of the discriminant, yielding three distinct cases.

\subsection{Damping Cases}
\begin{center}
    \begin{tabular}{|c|c|c|}
    \hline
    Case & Condition & Behaviour \\
    \hline
    Underdamped   & $\Gamma < 2\omega_0$ & Oscillatory, decaying envelope \\
    \hline
    Critically damped & $\Gamma = 2\omega_0$ & Fastest non-oscillatory return \\
    \hline
    Overdamped    & $\Gamma > 2\omega_0$ & Slow, non-oscillatory decay \\
    \hline
    \end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsubsection{Underdamped ($\Gamma < 2\omega_0$).}
The discriminant is negative, giving complex roots. The solution is a sinusoid with exponentially decaying envelope:
\[I(t) = \frac{V_0}{L\omega_f}\sin(\omega_f t)\,e^{-\Gamma t/2},\]
where the damped frequency is
\[\omega_f = \sqrt{\omega_0^2 - \left(\frac{\Gamma}{2}\right)^2}.\]
This is the most physically interesting case, exhibiting oscillatory behaviour before settling. As $R \to 0$, damping vanishes, $\omega_f \to \omega_0$, and in the unphysical limit of a pure LC circuit the oscillation persists indefinitely at the natural frequency $\omega_0$.

\subsubsection{Overdamped ($\Gamma > 2\omega_0$).}
The discriminant is positive, giving two distinct real roots. The solution decays without oscillation:
\[I(t) = \frac{V_0}{L\omega_f'}\sinh(\omega_f' t)\,e^{-\Gamma t/2},\]
where
\[\omega_f' = \sqrt{\left(\frac{\Gamma}{2}\right)^2 - \omega_0^2}.\]

\subsubsection{Critically damped ($\Gamma = 2\omega_0$).}
The discriminant is zero, giving a repeated real root. The solution is
\[I(t) = \frac{V_0}{L}\,t\,e^{-\Gamma t/2}.\]
This case represents the fastest return to equilibrium without oscillation.

\subsection{RLC Circuit with AC Driving}
\marginpar{February 19, 2026}
Consider the following series RLC circuit driven by $V(t) = V_0\cos(\omega t)$:
\begin{center}
    \begin{tikzpicture}[transform shape]
        \node[sground] at (-0.25, -1.5){};
        \draw (-1.25, 2) to[american resistor] (0.75, 2);
        \draw (0.75, 2) to[cute inductor] (2.75, 2);
        \draw (2.75, 2) to[capacitor] (2.75, -1.5);
        \draw (-2.25, 1.5) -| (-2.25, 2) -- (-1.25, 2);
        \draw (-2.25, -1.5) -- (2.75, -1.5);
        \draw (-2.25, -1.5) to[sinusoidal current source] (-2.25, 2);
        \node[anchor=east] at (-2.4, 0.25){$V(t) = V_0\cos(\omega t)$};
        \node[currarrow, rotate=-90] at (2.75, 0.75){};
        \node[anchor=west] at (2.9, 0.75){$I(t)$};
    \end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}
With $\tilde{V} = V_0 e^{j\omega t}$, $\tau = L/R$, and $\omega_0 = 1/\sqrt{LC}$, the total impedance is $Z_{\text{tot}} = R + j\omega L + \frac{1}{j\omega C}$, giving complex current
\[\tilde{I} = \frac{\tilde{V}}{Z_{\text{tot}}} = \frac{\tilde{V}}{R}\cdot\frac{j\omega\tau}{1 - \dfrac{\omega^2}{\omega_0^2} + j\omega\tau}.\]
The amplitude of the current is
\[|\tilde{I}|^2 = \left(\frac{V_0}{R}\right)^2 \frac{(\omega\tau)^2}{\left(1 - \dfrac{\omega^2}{\omega_0^2}\right)^2 + (\omega\tau)^2}.\]
At $\omega = \omega_0$ the denominator reduces to $(\omega_0\tau)^2$, so $|\tilde{I}|$ reaches its maximum $I_{\max} = V_0/R$. At resonance, the capacitive and inductive impedances cancel exactly, leaving only $R$.\\
We can obtain the phase offset by multiplying through by the complex conjugate of the denominator,
\[\frac{\tilde{I}}{\tilde{V}/R} = \frac{(\omega\tau)^2 + j\omega\tau\!\left(1 - \dfrac{\omega^2}{\omega_0^2}\right)}{\left(1 - \dfrac{\omega^2}{\omega_0^2}\right)^2 + (\omega\tau)^2}, \qquad \tan\phi = \frac{1 - \dfrac{\omega^2}{\omega_0^2}}{\omega\tau}.\]
The physical current is therefore $I(t) = |\tilde{I}|\cos(\omega t + \phi)$.

% ------- 8.5 Quality Factor -------
\subsection{Quality Factor}
\begin{definition}[Quality Factor]
    The quality factor $Q$ is a dimensionless parameter that quantifies the sharpness of a resonance peak in a resonant system. A high $Q$ corresponds to a narrow resonance.
\end{definition}
\begin{proposition}[Quality Factor of an RLC Circuit]
    The quality factor can be defined as
    \[Q = \frac{1}{\omega_0\tau} = \frac{\omega_0 L}{R}.\]
    As $R \to 0$, the circuit approaches a pure LC resonator, $Q \to \infty$, and only oscillation at $\omega_0$ can be sustained. At resonance the capacitive and inductive reactances are equal in magnitude but opposite in phase (offset by $\pi$ radians), so they cancel exactly and the circuit presents purely resistive impedance to the driving source.
\end{proposition}

% ------- 8.6 Power and Resonance -------
\subsection{Power and Resonance}
\begin{proposition}[Instantaneous Power]
    The instantaneous power delivered to the circuit is
    \[P(t) = V(t)\,I(t) = V_0 I_0\cos(\omega t)\cos(\omega t + \phi).\]
\end{proposition}
Time-averaging over one complete cycle gives
\[\langle P \rangle = \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T P(t)\,dt = \frac{V_0 I_0}{2}\cos\phi.\]
\begin{definition}[Power Factor]
    The factor $\cos\phi$ from the previous equation is called the power factor.
\end{definition}
\begin{remark}
    Capacitors and inductors introduce a phase offset $\phi$ that reduces the efficiency of power delivery. In a pure LC circuit the phase offset is necessarily $\pm\pi/2$, so $\cos\phi = 0$ and the time-averaged power input vanishes --- no net energy is delivered unless the circuit is driven exactly at resonance $\omega = \omega_0$, where the inductive and capacitive impedances cancel and $\phi = 0$.
\end{remark}

%CONTINUE FROM HERE
\subsection{Capacitor Voltage in a Driven RLC Circuit}
\marginpar{March 10, 2026}

For the driven series RLC circuit of Section~8.4, the voltage drop across the capacitor is
\[\Delta V_C = \frac{Q}{C} = \frac{1}{C}\int_0^t I(t')\,dt'.\]
Using the complex current $\tilde{I} = |\tilde{I}|e^{j(\omega t + \phi)}$, integrating gives
\[\tilde{V}_C = \frac{\tilde{I}}{j\omega C} = \frac{V_0}{\sqrt{\left(1 - \dfrac{\omega^2}{\omega_0^2}\right)^2 + (\omega\tau)^2}}\,e^{j(\omega t + \phi - \pi/2)}.\]
Equivalently, from the impedance divider,
\[\frac{\tilde{V}'}{\tilde{V}} = \frac{Z_C}{Z_{\text{tot}}} = \frac{1}{1 - \dfrac{\omega^2}{\omega_0^2} + j\omega\tau},\]
with amplitude and phase
\[\left|\frac{\tilde{V}'}{\tilde{V}}\right|^2 = \frac{1}{\left(1 - \dfrac{\omega^2}{\omega_0^2}\right)^2 + (\omega\tau)^2}, \qquad \phi = -\arctan\!\left(\frac{\omega\tau}{1 - \dfrac{\omega^2}{\omega_0^2}}\right).\]

The capacitor voltage peaks near, but slightly below, $\omega_0$, because the peak is determined by the combination of maximum current flow and the frequency-dependent capacitive reactance, rather than by maximising the current alone.

\subsubsection{Limiting Behaviour}

\paragraph{Low frequency ($\omega \ll \omega_0$).}
The capacitive impedance dominates and nearly all of the input voltage is dropped across the capacitor. The output amplitude approaches $V_0$ and the phase offset approaches zero.

\paragraph{At resonance ($\omega = \omega_0$).}
Current is limited only by $R$ and reaches its maximum. Large voltage drops appear across both the inductor and capacitor, but they cancel. The current is in phase with $V(t)$; since the capacitor voltage lags the current by $\pi/2$, it lags the input by $\pi/2$.

\paragraph{High frequency ($\omega \gg \omega_0$).}
The voltage drops almost entirely across the inductor, so $V(t) \approx L\frac{dI}{dt}$, giving
\[I(t) \approx \frac{1}{L}\int V(t')\,dt' = \frac{V_0}{\omega L}\sin(\omega t).\]
The capacitor voltage is then
\[\Delta V_C = \frac{1}{C}\int I(t')\,dt' \approx -\frac{V_0}{\omega^2 LC}\cos(\omega t) = -\left(\frac{\omega_0}{\omega}\right)^2 V(t).\]
The inductor current lags $V(t)$ by $\pi/2$; the capacitor voltage lags the current by a further $\pi/2$, so the capacitor voltage lags $V(t)$ by $\pi$ in total.

\begin{remark}
    The \textbf{natural frequency} $\omega_0$ is the inherent rate at which energy oscillates between the inductor and capacitor in the absence of external driving. The \textbf{resonant frequency} is the specific driving frequency that produces maximum current. For low damping these are nearly identical; for large resistance they diverge.
\end{remark}

% ------- 8.8 Quality Factor -------
\subsection{Quality Factor}

The quality factor $Q_f$ is a dimensionless measure of the ratio of energy stored to energy dissipated per cycle:
\[Q_f = 2\pi\,\frac{W_s}{W_L},\]
where $W_s$ is the energy stored in the circuit and $W_L$ is the energy lost as heat per cycle.

\subsubsection{Derivation for a Series RLC Circuit}
Taking $I(t) = I_0\cos(\omega_0 t)$, the maximum energy stored (in the inductor at peak current) is
\[W_s = \tfrac{1}{2}LI_0^2.\]
By Joule's law\footnote{Joule's heating law states that the heat produced in a conductor is proportional to the square of the current, the resistance, and the duration of flow.}, the energy dissipated per cycle $T_0 = 2\pi/\omega_0$ is
\[W_L = R\int_0^{T_0} I_0^2\cos^2(\omega_0 t)\,dt = \frac{RI_0^2 T_0}{2} = \frac{\pi R I_0^2}{\omega_0}.\]
Therefore,
\[Q_f = 2\pi\cdot\frac{\tfrac{1}{2}LI_0^2}{\dfrac{\pi R I_0^2}{\omega_0}} = \frac{\omega_0 L}{R} = \frac{1}{\omega_0\tau}.\]

% ------- 8.9 Quality Factor and Energy Decay -------
\subsection{Quality Factor and Energy Decay}

The quality factor is also directly related to the rate at which energy drains from a freely discharging RLC circuit. The current amplitude decays as
\[I_0(t) = I_0(0)\,e^{-\Gamma t/2}, \qquad \Gamma = \frac{R}{L},\]
so the stored energy decays as
\[E(t) = \tfrac{1}{2}LI_0^2(t) = E_0\,e^{-\Gamma t} \implies \frac{dE}{dt} = -\Gamma E.\]
The number of radians of oscillation completed per energy $e$-folding time is
\[Q_f = \omega_0\,\frac{E}{|dE/dt|} = \frac{\omega_0}{\Gamma} = \frac{\omega_0 L}{R} = \frac{1}{\omega_0\tau},\]
consistent with the result above. A high $Q_f$ therefore means the circuit loses a small fraction of its stored energy per oscillation cycle.
% ------- 8.10 Power Input into a Driven RLC Series Circuit -------
\subsection{Power Input into a Driven RLC Series Circuit}
\marginpar{March 12, 2026}

The instantaneous power delivered to the circuit is $P(t) = V(t)I(t)$, where $V(t) = V_0\cos(\omega t)$ and $I(t) = I_0(\omega)\cos(\omega t + \phi(\omega))$. Time-averaging over one cycle gives
\[\langle P(\omega)\rangle = \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T V(t)\,I(t)\,dt = \frac{V_0 I_0(\omega)}{2}\cos\phi(\omega) = \frac{V_0^2}{2R}\cdot\frac{(\omega\tau)^2}{\left(1 - \dfrac{\omega^2}{\omega_0^2}\right)^2 + (\omega\tau)^2}.\]
Substituting $x \equiv \omega/\omega_0$ and $Q_f = \omega_0 L/R = 1/(\omega_0\tau)$, this becomes
\[\langle P(x)\rangle = P_{\max}\cdot\frac{\dfrac{x^2}{Q_f^2}}{\left(1 - x^2\right)^2 + \dfrac{x^2}{Q_f^2}}, \qquad P_{\max} \equiv \frac{V_0^2}{2R}.\]
The power curves are normalised by $P_{\max}$, which itself depends on $R$ and hence on $Q_f$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High $Q_f$:} more power can be stored in the circuit, but only over a narrow range of driving frequencies.
    \item \textbf{Low $Q_f$:} less power is stored, but the circuit responds over a broader frequency range.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Steady State}
A driven RLC circuit does not accumulate energy indefinitely. The power input from the driving source is balanced by the power dissipated in the resistor, leading to a stable steady-state energy level. While the inductor and capacitor continuously exchange energy, the resistor converts electrical energy to heat, preventing unlimited accumulation. In the idealised limit $R \to 0$, physical effects such as inductor saturation, capacitor breakdown, or wire melting would prevent infinite energy storage in practice.

\subsubsection{Bandwidth and Quality Factor}
Define the \textbf{bandwidth} $\Delta\omega = \omega_2 - \omega_1$ as the frequency interval between the two half-power points, i.e.\ the frequencies at which $\langle P\rangle = P_{\max}/2$. Then
\[Q_f = \frac{\omega_0}{\Delta\omega}.\]
This relation is a general property of resonators and is not specific to RLC circuits. In the context of radio receivers, the RLC circuit acts as a tuner: power delivered from incoming radio waves is maximised at the resonant frequency, while signals at other frequencies are suppressed. A high $Q_f$ allows the receiver to distinguish closely spaced stations; a low $Q_f$ gives a broader but less selective response.

\section{Circuit Network Analysis}

\subsection{Kirchhoff's Laws (Review)}

\begin{definition}[Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL)]
    At any junction in a circuit, the sum of all currents entering equals the sum of all currents leaving.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL)]
    Around any closed loop in a circuit, the algebraic sum of all voltage changes equals zero.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Terminology}

\begin{definition}[Node]
    Any point in a circuit where two or more components connect.
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Junction (Principal Node)]
    A point where three or more branches meet. All junctions are nodes, but not all nodes are junctions. Nodes describe general connectivity; junctions are the key locations for analysing current division.
\end{definition}
\begin{definition}[Branch]
    A single path connecting two nodes, containing one or more circuit elements in series.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Methods of Analysis}

Kirchhoff's laws are straightforward to state but require systematic application in complex circuits. Three standard methods exist.

\subsubsection{Branch Method}
A current variable is assigned to each branch; KCL is written for each junction and KVL for each independent loop. If there are $n$ unknown branch currents and $k$ junctions, this produces $k - 1$ independent KCL equations and $n - k + 1$ independent KVL equations. This is the most general method but typically yields the largest system of simultaneous equations, making it inefficient for complex networks.

\subsubsection{Loop Current Method}
A single circulating current is assigned to each independent loop. KVL is applied to each loop; KCL is automatically satisfied. This method is best suited to circuits with many series elements or voltage sources. It can only be applied to \textbf{planar circuits}\footnote{A planar circuit is one that can be drawn on a flat surface with no crossing wires. Non-planar circuits contain branches that cannot be laid out in a plane without intersections, and the loop method cannot be applied directly to them.}.

\subsubsection{Nodal Method}
Voltage levels at each node are taken as the unknowns, with one node designated as the reference (ground). KCL is then applied at each non-reference node, expressing branch currents in terms of node voltages via Ohm's law (or its complex generalisation for AC circuits). The number of equations is reduced to the number of nodes minus one. This method is best suited to circuits with many parallel elements or current sources.

\begin{center}
    \begin{tabular}{|l|l|l|}
    \hline
    Method & Best suited for & Equations required \\
    \hline
    Branch    & General circuits & $n$ (largest system) \\
    Loop      & Many series elements / voltage sources & Independent loops only \\
    Nodal     & Many parallel elements / current sources & Nodes $- 1$ \\
    \hline
    \end{tabular}
\end{center}

% ------- 9.4 Superposition Theorem -------
\subsection{Superposition Theorem}
\marginpar{March 17, 2026}

\begin{theorem}[Superposition]
    In any circuit containing only linear elements, the current at any point is equal to the algebraic sum of the currents produced by each independent source acting alone. To find the contribution of a single voltage source, all other voltage sources are replaced by short circuits (plain wires) and all other current sources are replaced by open circuits.
\end{theorem}

% ------- 9.5 Current Sources -------
\subsection{Current Sources}

Just as a voltage source maintains a fixed potential difference regardless of the current drawn, a \textbf{current source} supplies a fixed current regardless of the voltage across it.

\begin{definition}[Ideal Current Source]
    An ideal current source supplies a fixed current that is completely independent of the voltage across its terminals, corresponding to an internal resistance of $r = \infty$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Real Current Source]
    A real current source has a finite internal resistance $r$, causing the delivered current to depend on the terminal voltage. It is modelled as an ideal current source in parallel with a resistance $r$.
\end{definition}

A current source can be realised in two ways:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Passive approximation:} Apply a voltage across a resistor whose resistance is much larger than the net resistance of the load to which the current is being supplied. The current through the load is then approximately constant at $I \approx V/r$.
    \item \textbf{Active implementation:} Use nonlinear active elements such as transistors to enforce a fixed current over a wide range of terminal voltages.
\end{itemize}

% ------- 9.6 Converting Between Source Types -------
\subsection{Converting Between Source Types}

To create a constant voltage source from a constant current source, a component is placed in parallel that shunts (diverts) excess current while maintaining a fixed voltage drop. This requires the shunt resistance to satisfy $r \ll R_L$, so that the terminal voltage remains approximately constant regardless of the load.

% ------- 9.7 Thévenin's Theorem -------
\subsection{Thévenin's Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Thévenin's Theorem]
    Any combination of voltage sources, current sources, and resistances with two terminals A and B is electrically equivalent to an ideal voltage source $e$ in series with a resistance $r$.
\end{theorem}

This theorem extends to circuits containing reactive elements (capacitors and inductors), in which case the equivalent circuit is an ideal AC voltage source in series with a complex impedance $Z$.

Since the Kirchhoff loop equations are linear, a linear relation must exist between the terminal voltage $\Delta V_{AB}$ and the current $I_{AB}$ drawn at A and returned at B:
\[\Delta V_{AB} = e - I_{AB}\,r.\]
In analogy with a real battery, the constant $e$ is the open-circuit voltage and $r$ is the series resistance. The negative sign reflects the fact that drawing current increases the voltage drop across internal resistive elements, reducing $\Delta V_{AB}$.

\subsubsection{Determining $e$ and $r$}

\paragraph{Approach 1: Experimental (black-box circuit).}
Connect a variable load resistor $R_L$ between terminals A and B and adjust it to vary $I_{AB}$. From the Thévenin relation,
\[I_{AB} = \frac{e}{r} - \frac{\Delta V_{AB}}{r}.\]
Alternatively, measure $\Delta V_{AB}$ as a function of $R_L$:
\[\Delta V_{AB} = e - r\,\frac{\Delta V_{AB}}{R_L} \implies \Delta V_{AB} = \frac{e\,R_L}{r + R_L} = \frac{e}{1 + \dfrac{r}{R_L}}.\]
Plotting $\Delta V_{AB}$ versus $R_L$ allows both $e$ and $r$ to be extracted from a fit.

\paragraph{Approach 2: Theoretical (known circuit diagram).}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Thévenin voltage} $e$: compute the open-circuit voltage between A and B with no current drawn ($I_{AB} = 0$).
    \item \textbf{Thévenin resistance} $r$: deactivate all independent sources (replace voltage sources with short circuits and current sources with open circuits), then compute the total resistance seen between terminals A and B.
\end{itemize}

% ------- 9.8 Norton's Theorem -------
\subsection{Norton's Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Norton's Theorem]
    Any combination of voltage sources, current sources, and resistances with two terminals A and B is electrically equivalent to an ideal current source $i$ in parallel with a resistance $r_N$.
\end{theorem}

\subsubsection{Equivalence of Thévenin and Norton Representations}

The two theorems are equivalent. Applying KCL at the output node of the Norton circuit,
\[i = I_{AB} + \frac{\Delta V_{AB}}{r_N} \implies \Delta V_{AB} = r_N\,i - r_N\,I_{AB}.\]
Identifying $e \equiv r_N\,i$ and $r \equiv r_N$ recovers the Thévenin relation $\Delta V_{AB} = e - r\,I_{AB}$. The conversion relations between the two representations are therefore
\[e = r_N\,i, \qquad r = r_N.\]

\subsection{Fourier Series}

Periodic signals can be expressed as sums of sinusoidal components of varying frequency. Let $f(t)$ be periodic with period $T$, so that $f(t) = f(t+T)$.

\begin{definition}[Fourier Series]
    The Fourier series of $f(t)$ is its expansion as a sum of cosine and sine functions:
    \[f(t) = \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\left[a_n\cos(\omega_n t) + b_n\sin(\omega_n t)\right],\]
    where $\omega_n = \frac{2\pi n}{T}$. The term $n = 1$ is the \textbf{fundamental}, $n = 2$ is the \textbf{first harmonic}, $n = 3$ is the \textbf{second harmonic}, and so on. The constant $a_0/2$ captures the non-varying (DC) component of the signal.
\end{definition}

Both sine and cosine terms are required in order to capture the phase of the periodic function being decomposed. Both families of functions are needed because neither alone forms a complete basis for arbitrary periodic signals.

\begin{example}
    $f(t) = \cos^2(at) = \tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2}\cos(2at)$, so $a_0 = 1$, $a_1 = \tfrac{1}{2}$, with $a = \pi/T$.
\end{example}

\begin{example}
    \begin{align*}
        f(t) = \cos^4(at) &= \left(\tfrac{1}{2} + \tfrac{1}{2}\cos(2at)\right)^2 \\
                          &= \tfrac{1}{4} + \tfrac{1}{2}\cos(2at) + \tfrac{1}{4}\cos^2(2at) \\
                          &= \tfrac{3}{8} + \tfrac{1}{2}\cos(2at) + \tfrac{1}{8}\cos(4at),
    \end{align*}
    so $a_0 = 3/4$, $a_1 = 1/2$, $a_2 = 1/8$, with $a = \pi/T$.
\end{example}

\subsection{Orthogonality Relations}

The cosine and sine functions satisfy the following orthogonality integrals, where $\delta_{nm}$ is the Kronecker delta:
\[\delta_{nm} = \begin{cases} 1, & n = m \\ 0, & n \neq m \end{cases}.\]
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\displaystyle\int_{-T/2}^{T/2}\cos\!\left(\tfrac{2\pi n}{T}t\right)\cos\!\left(\tfrac{2\pi m}{T}t\right)dt = \frac{T}{2}\,\delta_{nm}$
    \item $\displaystyle\int_{-T/2}^{T/2}\sin\!\left(\tfrac{2\pi n}{T}t\right)\sin\!\left(\tfrac{2\pi m}{T}t\right)dt = \frac{T}{2}\,\delta_{nm}$
    \item $\displaystyle\int_{-T/2}^{T/2}\cos\!\left(\tfrac{2\pi n}{T}t\right)\sin\!\left(\tfrac{2\pi m}{T}t\right)dt = 0$
\end{enumerate}
These relations express the fact that the cosines and sines form a \textbf{complete orthogonal basis} for periodic functions on $[-T/2,\, T/2]$.

\subsection{Fourier Coefficients}

Using the orthogonality relations, the Fourier coefficients are extracted by projection onto each basis function:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\displaystyle a_n = \frac{2}{T}\int_{-T/2}^{T/2} f(t)\cos(\omega_n t)\,dt$
    \item $\displaystyle b_n = \frac{2}{T}\int_{-T/2}^{T/2} f(t)\sin(\omega_n t)\,dt$
    \item $\displaystyle a_0 = \frac{2}{T}\int_{-T/2}^{T/2} f(t)\,dt$
\end{enumerate}
The constant $a_0$ is simply twice the time-average of $f(t)$ over one period.

\subsection{Convergence: Dirichlet's Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Dirichlet's Theorem]
    The Fourier series converges to $f(t)$ at all points where $f(t)$ is continuous. At a jump discontinuity, the series converges to the midpoint of the jump.
\end{theorem}

The theorem holds if and only if on the interval $[-T/2,\, T/2]$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $f(t)$ is single-valued.
    \item $f(t)$ has a finite number of maxima and minima.
    \item $f(t)$ has a finite number of discontinuities.
    \item $\displaystyle\int_{-T/2}^{T/2}|f(t)|^2\,dt$ is finite.
\end{enumerate}
Any physically realisable waveform satisfies these conditions.

\subsection{Fourier Transform}

The Fourier series applies to periodic signals. For aperiodic signals, the generalisation is the \textbf{Fourier transform}, which decomposes a signal into a continuous spectrum of frequencies.

\begin{definition}[Fourier Transform]
    \[F(\omega) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(t)\,e^{-j\omega t}\,dt.\]
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Inverse Fourier Transform]
    \[f(t) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} F(\omega)\,e^{j\omega t}\,d\omega.\]
\end{definition}

Here $f(t)$ is the \textbf{time-domain} representation of the signal, and $F(\omega)$ is its \textbf{frequency-domain} representation, describing how the signal's energy is distributed across frequencies.

% ------- 10.6 Gibbs Phenomenon -------
\subsection{Gibbs Phenomenon}

At a jump discontinuity, truncating the Fourier series at a finite number of terms $N$ produces periodic overshoots and undershoots near the jump. These do not decrease in height as $N$ increases — the overshoot remains approximately $9\%$ of the jump — but the \emph{width} of the oscillations shrinks to zero as $N \to \infty$. This is known as the \textbf{Gibbs phenomenon}.

\begin{remark}
    Gibbs phenomenon does not contradict Dirichlet's theorem. The theorem guarantees convergence to the midpoint of the jump in the limit $N \to \infty$; it makes no claim about the behaviour of partial sums at finite $N$.
\end{remark}

% ------- 10.7 Example: Sawtooth Wave -------
\subsection{Example: Sawtooth Wave}

Consider $f(t) = t/T$ for $|t| \leq T/2$. Since $f(t)$ is odd, $a_n = 0$ for all $n$. The sine coefficients are
\[b_n = \frac{2}{T}\int_{-T/2}^{T/2} f(t)\sin(\omega_n t)\,dt = \frac{2}{T^2}\int_{-T/2}^{T/2} t\sin\!\left(\frac{2\pi n t}{T}\right)dt = -\frac{(-1)^n}{n\pi}.\]

% ------- 10.8 Even and Odd Functions -------
\subsection{Even and Odd Functions}

The parity of $f(t)$ simplifies the computation of Fourier coefficients, since the product of two functions of the same parity is even, and of opposite parity is odd. An integral of an odd function over a symmetric interval vanishes.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item If $f(t)$ is \textbf{even} ($f(-t) = f(t)$):
    \begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
        \item $\displaystyle a_n = \frac{2}{T}\int_{-T/2}^{T/2} f(t)\cos(\omega_n t)\,dt = \frac{4}{T}\int_0^{T/2} f(t)\cos(\omega_n t)\,dt$ \quad (even $\times$ even $=$ even)
        \item $\displaystyle b_n = \frac{2}{T}\int_{-T/2}^{T/2} f(t)\sin(\omega_n t)\,dt = 0$ \quad (even $\times$ odd $=$ odd)
    \end{enumerate}
    \item If $f(t)$ is \textbf{odd} ($f(-t) = -f(t)$):
    \begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
        \item $\displaystyle a_n = \frac{2}{T}\int_{-T/2}^{T/2} f(t)\cos(\omega_n t)\,dt = 0$ \quad (odd $\times$ even $=$ odd)
        \item $\displaystyle b_n = \frac{2}{T}\int_{-T/2}^{T/2} f(t)\sin(\omega_n t)\,dt = \frac{4}{T}\int_0^{T/2} f(t)\sin(\omega_n t)\,dt$ \quad (odd $\times$ odd $=$ even)
    \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

% ------- 10.9 Transfer Functions and Linear Systems -------
\subsection{Transfer Functions and Linear Systems}

\begin{definition}[Transfer Function]
    For a linear circuit, the transfer function $H(\omega)$ is defined as the ratio of the complex output to the complex input for a single sinusoidal component:
    \[H(\omega) \equiv \frac{\tilde{V}_{\text{out}}}{\tilde{V}_{\text{in}}}.\]
\end{definition}

For a sinusoidal input $\tilde{V}_{\text{in}}(t) = V_0 e^{j\omega t}$, the circuit produces
\[\tilde{V}_{\text{out}}(t) = H(\omega)\,V_0\,e^{j\omega t}.\]
For the RC low-pass circuit, $H(\omega) = \dfrac{Z_C}{Z_R + Z_C} = \dfrac{1}{1 + j\omega RC}$, giving
\[|H(\omega)| = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + (\omega\tau)^2}}, \qquad \phi(\omega) = -\arctan(\omega\tau).\]
For a real cosine input $V_{\text{in}}(t) = V_0\cos(\omega t + \phi_0)$, the output is
\[V_{\text{out}}(t) = |H(\omega)|\,V_0\cos\!\left(\omega t + \phi_0 + \phi(\omega)\right).\]

\subsubsection{Application to a Sawtooth Input}

Since the circuit is linear, it acts independently on each Fourier component. For a sawtooth input
\[V_{\text{in}}(t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} b_n \sin(\omega_n t),\]
the output is
\[V_{\text{out}}(t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} b_n\,|H(\omega_n)|\sin\!\left(\omega_n t + \phi(\omega_n)\right),\]
where
\[b_n = -\frac{(-1)^n}{n\pi}, \qquad |H(\omega_n)| = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + (\omega_n\tau)^2}}, \qquad \phi(\omega_n) = -\arctan(\omega_n\tau).\]

% ------- 10.10 Complex Form of the Fourier Series -------
\subsection{Complex Form of the Fourier Series}

Using Euler's relation, the Fourier series can be rewritten in terms of complex exponentials:
\[f(t) = \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\left[a_n\cos(\omega_n t) + b_n\sin(\omega_n t)\right] = \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} c_k\,e^{j\omega_k t}, \qquad \omega_k = \frac{2\pi k}{T},\]
where
\[c_0 = \frac{a_0}{2}, \qquad c_m = \frac{a_m - jb_m}{2}, \qquad c_{-m} = \frac{a_m + jb_m}{2}, \quad m \in \mathbb{N}.\]
The complex exponentials satisfy an orthogonality relation,
\[\int_{-T/2}^{T/2} e^{j\omega_k t}\,e^{-j\omega_{k'} t}\,dt = T\,\delta_{kk'},\]
from which the coefficients are extracted by
\[c_k = \frac{1}{T}\int_{-T/2}^{T/2} f(t)\,e^{-j\omega_k t}\,dt, \qquad k \in \mathbb{Z}.\]

\begin{example}
    Let $f(t) = \begin{cases} 1, & |t| \leq T/4 \\ 0, & T/4 < |t| \leq T/2 \end{cases}$. Then
    \[c_k = \frac{1}{T}\int_{-T/4}^{T/4} e^{-j\omega_k t}\,dt = \frac{1}{2j\pi k}\!\left(e^{j\pi k/2} - e^{-j\pi k/2}\right) = \frac{\sin(\pi k/2)}{\pi k}.\]
    The coefficients are real and symmetric ($c_k = c_{-k}$), which reflects the fact that $f(t)$ is a real-valued even function.
\end{example}

\subsection{Fourier Series vs.\ Fourier Transform}

    \hline

% ------- 10.12 From Fourier Series to Fourier Transform -------
\subsection{From Fourier Series to Fourier Transform}

\subsubsection{Negative Frequencies}
In a Fourier series or Fourier transform, negative frequencies represent the clockwise rotation of complex phasors ($e^{-j\omega t}$), indicating a decreasing phase angle. They are the necessary mathematical counterpart to positive frequencies, ensuring that imaginary parts cancel when reconstructing real-valued signals.

\subsubsection{The Limiting Argument}
As the period $T$ of a periodic function is stretched to infinity, the discrete harmonics in the frequency domain become more and more closely spaced, eventually forming a continuous function of frequency. This is the origin of the Fourier transform.

Define $\omega_k = \frac{2\pi k}{T}$, $c(\omega_k) \equiv c_k$, $F(\omega) \equiv T c(\omega)$, and $\Delta\omega \equiv \frac{2\pi}{T}$. Then
\[F(\omega_k) = T c(\omega_k) = \int_{-T/2}^{T/2} f(t)\,e^{-j\omega_k t}\,dt,\]
and the Fourier series can be rewritten as
\[f(t) = \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} c_k\,e^{j\omega_k t} = \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{F(\omega_k)}{T}\,e^{j\omega_k t} = \frac{1}{2\pi}\sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} F(\omega_k)\,e^{j\omega_k t}\,\Delta\omega.\]
Taking the limit $T \to \infty$, the discrete spacing $\Delta\omega \to d\omega$ and the sum becomes an integral:
\[F(\omega) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(t)\,e^{-j\omega t}\,dt, \qquad f(t) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} F(\omega)\,e^{j\omega t}\,d\omega.\]
A Fourier series uses a discrete sum because periodic functions contain only specific harmonics; the continuous integral of the Fourier transform acts as an orthogonal projection that isolates the contribution of each frequency.

% ------- 10.13 Fourier Transform Pairs -------
\subsection{Fourier Transform Pairs}

The forward and inverse transforms form a symmetric pair:
\[f(t) \xrightarrow{\mathcal{F}} F(\omega) \qquad \text{(forward)}, \qquad F(\omega) \xrightarrow{\mathcal{F}^{-1}} f(t) \qquad \text{(inverse)}.\]
In this nomenclature, the Fourier series coefficients $c_k$ play the role of the inverse transform evaluated at discrete frequencies.

\subsubsection{Example: Fourier Transform of a Box Function}
Let $f(t) = 1$ for $|t| \leq a$ and zero otherwise. Then
\[F(\omega) = \int_{-a}^{a} e^{-j\omega t}\,dt = \frac{e^{j\omega a} - e^{-j\omega a}}{j\omega} = 2a\,\frac{\sin(\omega a)}{\omega a} = 2a\,\operatorname{sinc}(\omega a),\]
where $\operatorname{sinc}(x) \equiv \sin(x)/x$. The width of the box and the width of its Fourier transform are inversely related: a wider box produces a narrower sinc function, and a narrower box produces a wider sinc function.

% ------- 10.14 Time-Bandwidth Product -------
\subsection{Time-Bandwidth Product}

The first zeros of $F(\omega) = 2a\,\operatorname{sinc}(\omega a)$ occur at $\omega = \pm\pi/a$. The width of the central peak in frequency space is therefore
\[\Delta\omega = \frac{\pi}{a} - \left(-\frac{\pi}{a}\right) = \frac{2\pi}{a}.\]
With $\Delta t = 2a$ denoting the width of the box in the time domain,
\[\Delta\omega \cdot \Delta t = 4\pi = \text{constant}.\]
This inverse relationship between time-domain and frequency-domain widths is a fundamental property of the Fourier transform, and is directly analogous to the Heisenberg uncertainty principle in quantum mechanics.

% ------- 10.15 Dirac Delta Function -------
\subsection{Dirac Delta Function}

As the box width $a \to \infty$, the function $f(t) \to 1$ everywhere, and its Fourier transform $F(\omega) = 2a\,\operatorname{sinc}(\omega a)$ concentrates into a single spike at $\omega = 0$. The normalisation follows from
\[\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} F(\omega)\,d\omega = 2a\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{\sin(\omega a)}{\omega a}\,d\omega = 2\pi.\]
This motivates the definition of the \textbf{Dirac delta function}:
\[\delta(\omega) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\lim_{a \to \infty} 2a\,\operatorname{sinc}(\omega a),\]
with the properties
\[\delta(\omega) = \begin{cases} \infty, & \omega = 0 \\ 0, & \omega \neq 0 \end{cases}, \qquad \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \delta(\omega)\,d\omega = 1, \qquad \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \delta(\omega)\,G(\omega)\,d\omega = G(0).\]

% ------- 10.16 Application: Pulse Input into an RC Circuit -------
\subsection{Application: Pulse Input into an RC Circuit}

Consider a rectangular voltage pulse of width $\Delta t$ as the input to the RC low-pass circuit. Its Fourier transform is
\[\tilde{V}(\omega) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} V(t)\,e^{-j\omega t}\,dt = \Delta t\,\frac{\sin\!\left(\omega\Delta t/2\right)}{\omega\Delta t/2}.\]
Expressing $V(t)$ as a superposition of frequency components and applying the transfer function $H(\omega)$ to each component independently (by linearity),
\[V'(t) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} H(\omega)\,\tilde{V}(\omega)\,e^{j\omega t}\,d\omega = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{\sin(\omega\Delta t/2)}{\omega}\cdot\frac{e^{j\omega t}}{1 + j\omega\tau}\,d\omega,\]
which can be evaluated numerically. As the pulse width increases, the ringing in the Fourier transform becomes sharper, the amplitude of $\tilde{V}(\omega)$ grows, and the sinc function narrows.

% ------- 10.17 Translated Dirac Delta Function -------
\subsection{Translated Dirac Delta Function}

The Dirac delta function shifted to $\omega = \omega_0$ is defined by
\[\delta(\omega - \omega_0) = \begin{cases} \infty, & \omega = \omega_0 \\ 0, & \omega \neq \omega_0 \end{cases}, \qquad \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \delta(\omega - \omega_0)\,d\omega = 1, \qquad \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \delta(\omega - \omega_0)\,G(\omega)\,d\omega = G(\omega_0).\]
Its inverse Fourier transform is
\[f(t) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \delta(\omega - \omega_0)\,e^{j\omega t}\,d\omega = \frac{1}{2\pi}\,e^{j\omega_0 t},\]
i.e.\ a shifted delta function in frequency space corresponds to a pure complex exponential in time.

% ------- 10.18 Fourier Transforms of Cosine and Sine -------
\subsection{Fourier Transforms of Cosine and Sine}

Using the translated delta function, the Fourier transforms of the elementary sinusoids follow directly.

\paragraph{Cosine.}
Writing $\cos(\omega_0 t) = \frac{e^{j\omega_0 t} + e^{-j\omega_0 t}}{2}$ and comparing with the inverse transform,
\[f(t) = \cos(\omega_0 t) \implies F(\omega) = \pi\left[\delta(\omega - \omega_0) + \delta(\omega + \omega_0)\right].\]

\paragraph{Sine.}
Writing $\sin(\omega_0 t) = \frac{e^{j\omega_0 t} - e^{-j\omega_0 t}}{2j}$,
\[f(t) = \sin(\omega_0 t) \implies F(\omega) = \frac{\pi}{j}\left[\delta(\omega - \omega_0) - \delta(\omega + \omega_0)\right].\]

\begin{remark}
    Even though $\sin(x)$ is an odd function, the magnitudes of its positive and negative frequency components are still symmetric, because it is a real-valued function. Furthermore, $\sin(x)$ and $\cos(x)$ are the same function shifted by $\pi/2$; observe how this phase shift is encoded in the Fourier transform (the factor of $1/j$ corresponds to a $-\pi/2$ phase). The power spectrum $|F(\omega)|^2$ would lose this phase information.
\end{remark}

% ------- 10.19 Fourier Transform Pairs Table -------
\subsection{Fourier Transform Pairs}

The following table summarises the key Fourier transform pairs. The correspondence is symmetric: if $f(t) \leftrightarrow F(\omega)$, then $F(t) \leftrightarrow 2\pi f(-\omega)$, so each row applies in both directions.

\begin{center}
{\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
\begin{tabular}{|c|c|}
    \hline
    \textbf{Time domain} $f(t)$ & \textbf{Frequency domain} $F(\omega)$ \\
    \hline
    Box function & $\operatorname{sinc}$ function (ringing) \\
    \hline
    Constant & Dirac delta $\delta(\omega)$ \\
    \hline
    Complex exponential $e^{j\omega_0 t}$ & Shifted delta $2\pi\delta(\omega - \omega_0)$ \\
    \hline
    Cosine $\cos(\omega_0 t)$ & Pair of deltas at $\pm\omega_0$ (real) \\
    \hline
    Sine $\sin(\omega_0 t)$ & Pair of deltas at $\pm\omega_0$ (imaginary) \\
    \hline
    Dirac delta $\delta(t)$ & Constant \\
    \hline
    $\operatorname{sinc}$ function & Box function \\
    \hline
    $\operatorname{sinc}^2$ function & Triangle function \\
    \hline
    Gaussian & Gaussian \\
    \hline
\end{tabular}}
\end{center}

A constant function is a sinusoid with infinite period (zero frequency), which is why its transform is a single spike at $\omega = 0$. A cosine gives a purely real Fourier transform; a sine gives a purely imaginary one. In general, the Fourier transform of a real-valued function is complex.

% ------- 10.20 Translation Theorems -------
\subsection{Translation Theorems}

\paragraph{Time translation.}
Shifting $f(t)$ by $t_0$ in the time domain multiplies its Fourier transform by a phase factor:
\[f(t - t_0) \xrightarrow{\mathcal{F}} e^{-j\omega t_0}\,F(\omega).\]
The amplitude spectrum $|F(\omega)|$ is unchanged; only the phase is affected.

\paragraph{Frequency translation.}
Shifting $F(\omega)$ by $\omega_0$ in the frequency domain corresponds to modulation in the time domain:
\[F(\omega - \omega_0) \xrightarrow{\mathcal{F}^{-1}} e^{j\omega_0 t}\,f(t).\]

% ------- 10.21 Example: Fourier Transform of a Pulse Pair -------
\subsection{Example: Fourier Transform of a Pulse Pair}

Consider the sum of two identical square pulses of width $\Delta t$, displaced symmetrically about the origin:
\[f(t) = f_p(t - t_0) + f_p(t + t_0),\]
where $f_p$ denotes a single square pulse with Fourier transform $F_p(\omega)$. By the time-translation theorem,
\[F(\omega) = e^{-j\omega t_0}F_p(\omega) + e^{j\omega t_0}F_p(\omega) = 2F_p(\omega)\cos(\omega t_0).\]
The result is a $\operatorname{sinc}$ function modulated by a cosine, producing an interference (beat) pattern in the frequency domain. This is directly analogous to the fringe pattern produced by two radio telescopes operating as an interferometer: the two spatially separated apertures play the role of the two time-displaced pulses.

% ------- 10.22 Reality Condition -------
\subsection{Reality Condition}

The Fourier transform can in general be complex, but the requirement that $f(t)$ be real-valued imposes a constraint on $F(\omega)$. If $f(t) \in \mathbb{R}$, then $f(t) = f^*(t)$, and it follows that
\[F^*(\omega) = F(-\omega).\]
That is, the Fourier transform of a real signal has Hermitian symmetry: the negative-frequency content is entirely determined by the positive-frequency content.

% ------- 10.23 Power Spectrum -------
\subsection{Power Spectrum}

\begin{definition}[Power Spectrum]
    The power spectrum of a signal $f(t)$ is
    \[|F(\omega)|^2 = F(\omega)\,F^*(\omega) = F(\omega)\,F(-\omega).\]
    The square (rather than the square root) is used because power is proportional to the square of amplitude, $P \propto A^2$.
\end{definition}

When plotting $|F(\omega)|^2$, phase information is lost and the spectrum is symmetric in $\omega$ (since $|F(-\omega)|^2 = |F(\omega)|^2$ for real signals).

\subsubsection{One-Sided Spectrum}
Since the power spectrum is symmetric, it is common to plot the \textbf{one-sided spectrum}: $2|F(\omega)|^2$ over positive $\omega$ only. The factor of two ensures that the total integrated power remains equal to that of the original two-sided spectrum, since the power from the discarded negative frequencies is folded back onto the positive side.

\begin{example}
    For $f(t) = \sin(\omega_0 t)$, the Fourier transform is
    \[F(\omega) = \frac{\pi}{j}\left[\delta(\omega - \omega_0) - \delta(\omega + \omega_0)\right],\]
    giving a power spectrum with two symmetric spikes at $\pm\omega_0$.
\end{example}

% ------- 10.24 Finite-Length Signals and Windowing -------
\subsection{Finite-Length Signals and Windowing}

The Fourier transform of a finite-duration signal is not an exact Dirac delta function: a finite sine wave has a defined duration in time, and therefore cannot be perfectly localised to a single frequency. In practice, a finite-length signal $f(t)$ is represented as the product of an infinite-duration signal $s(t)$ and a \textbf{window function} $w(t)$:
\[F(\omega) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} w(t)\,s(t)\,e^{-j\omega t}\,dt.\]
Multiplying the signal by a smooth window function suppresses the side lobes that arise from the sharp truncation, at the expense of a modest increase in bandwidth (i.e.\ a broadening of the central peak).

% ------- 10.25 Discrete Sampling and the Nyquist Criterion -------
\subsection{Discrete Sampling and the Nyquist Criterion}

Experimental signals are necessarily measured at discrete time points $t_n = n\,\Delta t_s$, where $n$ is an integer and $\Delta t_s$ is the sampling interval. The corresponding \textbf{sampling frequency} is $f_s = 1/\Delta t_s$.

\begin{theorem}[Nyquist Criterion]
    To faithfully reconstruct a signal, the sampling frequency must be at least twice the maximum frequency present in the signal:
    \[f_s \geq 2f_{\max} \implies f_{\max} \leq \frac{f_s}{2} = \frac{1}{2\,\Delta t_s}.\]
    The maximum detectable frequency $f_{\max} = f_s/2$ is called the \textbf{Nyquist frequency}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{example}
    To detect a pulsar with period $P = 1\,\text{ms}$, the time resolution must be at most $\Delta t_s = 0.5\,\text{ms}$. To also resolve its fourth harmonic (period $P/4 = 0.25\,\text{ms}$), the sampling interval must be reduced to $\Delta t_s \leq 0.125\,\text{ms}$.
\end{example}

% ------- 10.26 Discrete Fourier Transform -------
\subsection{Discrete Fourier Transform}

For a discretely sampled signal of $N$ points, the \textbf{Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT)} is
\[F(k) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} f(n)\,e^{-j2\pi nk/N}.\]
For a discrete sine wave with sampling interval $\Delta t$, period $P$, and total observation time $T_{\max} = N\,\Delta t$, the DFT produces:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A peak at the fundamental angular frequency $\omega_0 = 2\pi/P$.
    \item A frequency resolution of $\Delta\omega \approx 2\pi/T_{\max}$ (finer resolution requires longer observation).
    \item A maximum detectable frequency of $\omega_{\max} \approx \pi/\Delta t$ (the Nyquist frequency in angular units).
\end{itemize}

% ------- 10.27 Amplitude Modulation -------
\subsection{Amplitude Modulation}

\begin{definition}[Amplitude Modulation (AM)]
    Amplitude modulation encodes a signal by varying the amplitude of a high-frequency carrier wave. If the carrier is $\cos(\omega_c t)$ and the baseband signal is $f(t)$, the modulated signal is
    \[s(t) = \left[1 + f(t)\right]\cos(\omega_c t).\]
    By the frequency-translation theorem, modulation shifts the spectrum of $f(t)$ to be centred on $\pm\omega_c$. AM radio uses this principle to transmit audio signals over radio-frequency carriers.
\end{definition}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Charge relation: $Q = CV$
    \item Capacitance: $C = \dfrac{\varepsilon A}{d}$
    \item Permittivity of free space: $\varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12}\,\text{F/m}$
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{RC Circuits}
(time constant $\tau = RC$)

Charging:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Voltage: $V_C(t) = V\!\left(1-e^{-t/\tau}\right)$
    \item Current: $i(t) = \dfrac{V}{R}e^{-t/\tau}$
    \item Charge: $Q(t) = CV\!\left(1-e^{-t/\tau}\right)$
\end{itemize}
At $t=0$, the capacitor behaves like a short circuit. As $t\to\infty$, it behaves like an open circuit.

Discharging:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Voltage: $V_C(t) = V_0\,e^{-t/\tau}$
    \item Current: $I(t) = I_0\,e^{-t/\tau}$
    \item Charge: $Q(t) = Q_0\,e^{-t/\tau}$
\end{itemize}

Stored energy: $W = \dfrac{1}{2}CV^2$

\subsubsection*{Inductors}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Inductor voltage: $V = L\,\dfrac{dI}{dt}$
    \item Inductance (solenoid): $L = \dfrac{\mu N^2 A}{l}$
    \item Permeability of free space: $\mu_0 = 4\pi \times 10^{-7}\,\mathrm{T} \cdot \mathrm{m/A}$
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{RL Circuits}
(time constant $\tau = L/R$)

Charging:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Voltage: $V_L(t) = V\,e^{-t/\tau}$
    \item Current: $i(t) = \dfrac{V}{R}\!\left(1-e^{-t/\tau}\right)$
\end{itemize}
At $t=0$, an inductor behaves like an open circuit. As $t\to\infty$, it behaves like a short circuit.

Discharging:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Voltage: $V_L(t) = V_0\,e^{-t/\tau}$
    \item Current: $I(t) = I_0\,e^{-t/\tau}$
\end{itemize}

Stored energy: $W = \dfrac{1}{2}LI^2$

\subsubsection*{Energy Dissipation}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Resistor: $U = \displaystyle\int R\,I^2(t)\,dt$
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{RLC Circuits}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Natural frequency: $\omega_0 = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{LC}}$
    \item Damping factor: $\Gamma = \dfrac{R}{L}$
\end{itemize}

Impedances:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Resistor: $Z_R = R$
    \item Capacitor: $Z_C = \dfrac{1}{j\omega C}$

\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Miscellaneous}

\textit{Transient}: The short initial response when a circuit is switched on, typically involving terms like $e^{-t/\tau}$.

\textit{Steady-state}: The long-term behaviour of the circuit after transients vanish.

Filtering rules:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{Low-pass filter}: Blocks high-frequency signals; allows low-frequency signals to pass. Often an RC circuit with output across the capacitor, or an LR circuit where the inductor appears first in the signal path.
    \item \textit{High-pass filter}: Blocks low-frequency/DC signals; allows high-frequency signals to pass. Commonly an RC circuit with output across the resistor, or an LR circuit where the resistor precedes the inductor.
\end{itemize}

Water analogy:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Resistor $\leftrightarrow$ friction; dissipates energy.
    \item Inductor $\leftrightarrow$ inertia; resists changes in current.
    \item Capacitor $\leftrightarrow$ spring; stores electrical energy.
\end{itemize}

Phase shifts (ICE vs ELI):
\begin{itemize}
    \item In a capacitor, current leads voltage (ICE).
    \item In an inductor, voltage leads current (ELI).
\end{itemize}

Damping cases in an RLC circuit:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{Underdamped}: Oscillatory behaviour before settling.
    \item \textit{Overdamped}: No oscillations; system slowly approaches equilibrium.
    \item \textit{Critically damped}: Fastest return to equilibrium without oscillations.
\end{itemize}

\textit{Integrator}: Circuit where the output is proportional to the integral of the input; associated with $\frac{1}{j\omega}$.

\textit{Differentiator}: Circuit where the output corresponds to the rate of change of the input; associated with $j\omega$.

\textit{Complex impedance $Z$}: Treat impedance like resistance when solving AC circuits using $V = IZ$.

Decibels (dB) for Bode plots:
\[\text{Gain (dB)} = 20\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{V_{\text{out}}}{V_{\text{in}}}\right).\]

$-3\,\text{dB}$ point: The cutoff where the signal power is reduced by half.

Cutoff frequency $\omega_c$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item RC circuit: $\omega_c = \dfrac{1}{RC}$
    \item LR circuit: $\omega_c = \dfrac{R}{L}$
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Tip}: For sinusoidal inputs, replace $L$ and $C$ with their impedance expressions and analyze the circuit using standard algebra instead of solving differential equations.
